    1 When to Use Causal Inference and AB testing? ▸

    we need to check if we can force the users to use the new feature(like a ui change, new ranking systme) to determine if we can use ab testing or causal inference/

    the statement above touches on a critical consideration for determining whether A/B testing or causal inference methods are feasible for evaluating the impact of a new feature. Here’s an expanded view:1. Forced Exposure to the New Feature:

    • If you can force users to use the new feature (e.g., by rolling it out to a subset of users while keeping others on the existing system), you can reliably conduct an A/B test. This is because:
    • You have control over who sees the new feature (treatment group) versus the old version (control group).
    • Randomization ensures that the two groups are comparable, enabling you to estimate the causal impact of the feature.2. Opt-In/Voluntary Use of the Feature:
    • If users can choose whether or not to use the new feature, it introduces selection bias, making a direct A/B test problematic. In such cases, users who adopt the feature may differ systematically from those who don’t, and the difference in outcomes might not be due to the feature itself.
    • To analyze such cases, you may need to rely on causal inference methods, such as:
    • Instrumental Variables: Using external factors to explain adoption.
    • Propensity Score Matching: Controlling for observable differences between adopters and non-adopters.
    • Difference-in-Differences: Comparing changes over time between groups.3. Scenarios Where A/B Testing Works Well:
    • The platform has full control over assigning users to see the new feature.
    • There’s no interference between treatment and control groups (e.g., users in the treatment group don’t influence outcomes for those in the control group).
    • Randomization is practical and ethical for rolling out the feature.4. Scenarios Requiring Causal Inference:
    • When the feature rollout cannot be randomized due to technical, ethical, or business constraints.
    • Users self-select into using the feature (e.g., opting into a beta program).
    • The rollout is gradual or determined by other external factors (e.g., region, user type).In summary, determining whether you can control user exposure to the new feature is key to deciding if A/B testing is feasible. If forced exposure is not possible, you’ll need to explore causal inference methods to account for confounding variables and estimate the feature’s causal effect.

    Broadly, causal inference is the field that seeks to determine whether Y would be different, absent X (different people frame this differently). A/B tests are one (powerful) way to determine this, because they randomize the treatment, so it will be uncorrelated with anything else (like your feature enhancement).

    Eg, you want to know if people spend more if the "buy" button is twice as big? For 30 days, half your visitors will see the bigger button, half see the original. At the end of 30 days, you take the purchase sizes for the original button people, the new button people, and do a t-test to see if the difference is significant.

    However, sometimes you can't randomize (and run an A/B test). Sometimes this is because you have "found" data (the data has already been collected), sometimes it's because randomizing the treatment is unethical (you can't make someone smoke for 30 years to learn if smoking causes cancer). In that case, you have to turn to the tools of "observational causal inference," which have been developed across statistics and the social sciences. A good free introduction is available here.

    When to Use Causal Inference:

    • Randomization is not feasible:
    • Ethical or practical constraints prevent random assignment (e.g., studying the effects of smoking on health).
    • Observational data is the only option (e.g., historical data or policy changes).
    • Long-term effects or rare events:
    • A/B tests may require a large sample size or long duration to capture rare outcomes.
    • Causal inference methods (e.g., regression discontinuity, propensity score matching, difference-in-differences) can leverage existing data for analysis.
    • Natural Experiments or Policy Changes:
    • When a policy or event provides a "natural randomization" (e.g., minimum wage changes, new tax laws), causal inference methods can estimate effects without conducting an A/B test.
    • Data from the past:
    • If the data is already collected (observational or historical), causal inference is the primary option.
    • Understanding mechanisms or mediators:
    • Causal inference often digs deeper into the "why" and "how" something happens, identifying pathways and mediating variables.

    When to Use A/B Testing:

    • Controlled experimentation is feasible:
    • You can randomize participants or subjects into treatment and control groups (e.g., testing website designs or email campaigns).
    • Short-term interventions:
    • A/B tests are ideal for immediate changes, like evaluating a marketing strategy or new product feature.
    • Clear and measurable outcomes:
    • The outcome metric is easy to define and measure (e.g., click-through rates, conversion rates).
    • Sufficient traffic or sample size:
    • A/B tests work best when you have enough participants to detect statistically significant effects.
    • Low risk of spillover or interference:
    • Treatment and control groups must not influence each other (e.g., one customer’s experience does not affect another's).

    Conclusion:

    Use causal inference when experimentation isn't possible or practical, or when you're working with observational data. Use A/B testing when you can randomize treatment in a controlled environment to directly test the impact of changes. In some cases, a combination of both can strengthen insights.

    Regression Discontinuity Design (RDD) is a quasi-experimental research design used to estimate the causal effect of an intervention or treatment. It is applied when there is a specific cutoff or threshold (e.g., score, age, income) that determines whether a unit (e.g., person, school, firm) receives a treatment. RDD helps estimate the treatment effect by comparing units that are just above and below the threshold, assuming that these units are similar in all respects except for the treatment.Key Components of RDD:

    • Running variable (also called the forcing variable): This is the variable that determines the assignment of treatment (e.g., test scores, age, income).
    • Cutoff: The threshold value of the running variable that determines whether a unit receives the treatment (e.g., students who score above 80 on a test might receive an intervention, while those below 80 do not).
    • Treatment: The intervention or program that units above the cutoff receive.Types of RDD:
    • Sharp RDD: In this case, the assignment to treatment is completely deterministic; units above the cutoff always get the treatment, and those below the cutoff never do.
    • Fuzzy RDD: Here, the assignment to treatment is not deterministic. Units above the cutoff are more likely to receive the treatment, but some may not, and some below the cutoff may still receive it.When to Use RDD:RDD is most useful when there is a clear, arbitrary cutoff that determines whether a unit gets treated. You use RDD when random assignment is not feasible, but you have access to the cutoff that can be leveraged to make comparisons. It is especially beneficial in situations where random control trials are not possible due to ethical or logistical constraints.Examples of RDD:
    • Education:
    • A university offers scholarships based on entrance exam scores, with students who score above a certain threshold (e.g., 85 out of 100) receiving the scholarship. To assess the impact of the scholarship on student performance, you can use RDD by comparing the outcomes of students who score just above and just below the cutoff.
    • Healthcare:
    • A public health program offers subsidies for healthcare to people whose income is below a certain threshold. RDD could be used to evaluate the impact of the subsidies by comparing the health outcomes of people who are just below and just above the income cutoff.
    • Policy Analysis:
    • A government implements a job training program for individuals who are unemployed for more than 6 months. Those who cross this 6-month threshold might receive training. To evaluate the impact of the training on employment outcomes, RDD could be used to compare individuals just above and below the 6-month unemployment mark.
    • Social Programs:
    • A country’s welfare program provides financial support to families below a certain income level. You could use RDD to assess the effects of the program on children's health or educational outcomes by comparing children in families just above and below the income cutoff.Assumptions of RDD:
    • Continuity: In RDD, the potential outcomes of a unit (i.e., what would have happened if they had not received the treatment) change smoothly around the cutoff. There should be no other discontinuities (except for the treatment) at the cutoff.
    • No manipulation of the running variable: Units should not be able to manipulate their score (or forcing variable) around the cutoff to receive the treatment.RDD is considered a powerful design for causal inference because, under the right conditions, it can approximate random assignment, making it a strong alternative to randomized control trials (RCTs) when randomization is not feasible.4o miniYou said:what tool you can use to conduct this?ChatGPT said:ChatGPTTo conduct Regression Discontinuity Design (RDD) analysis, several statistical and data analysis tools can be used.     nterpreting the results of a Regression Discontinuity Design (RDD) analysis involves understanding the treatment effect at the cutoff and checking whether the assumptions behind the design hold. Here's a structured approach to interpreting RDD results:1. Treatment Effect Estimate:

    • Point Estimate of the Treatment Effect:
    • The primary output of an RDD is the estimate of the causal treatment effect, which is the difference in the outcome variable between units just above and just below the cutoff.
    • This estimate represents the local average treatment effect (LATE), meaning it estimates the effect of the treatment for those units close to the cutoff (e.g., students near a passing score threshold or individuals just above the income cutoff for welfare).
    • Interpretation:
    • If the treatment (e.g., scholarship, healthcare program) is assigned to individuals above the cutoff, the estimated treatment effect tells you how the outcome of interest (e.g., test scores, health) changes for individuals just above the cutoff compared to those just below the cutoff, who do not receive the treatment.
    • A significant and positive treatment effect means that the treatment improves the outcome, while a negative or insignificant effect suggests no or detrimental impact.Example: If you are estimating the impact of a scholarship program and the treatment effect is +5 points on a test score, it means that students just above the threshold (who received the scholarship) scored, on average, 5 points higher than students just below the cutoff (who did not receive the scholarship).2. Checking for Continuity:
    • Graphical Check:
    • A key assumption in RDD is that, in the absence of the treatment, the relationship between the running variable (e.g., test score, age) and the outcome would be continuous at the cutoff.
    • A visual check using scatter plots or line graphs is essential. Plot the outcome variable against the running variable and visually inspect whether there is a discontinuity (jump) at the cutoff.
    • If the graph shows a sharp jump at the cutoff, it suggests a valid RDD and the treatment might have caused the observed discontinuity in the outcome.Example: A sharp increase in test scores at the cutoff could indicate that the scholarship program led to better performance for those who received it.3. Bandwidth Selection:
    • Bandwidth refers to how far from the cutoff you include units in the analysis. A narrower bandwidth includes only those units very close to the cutoff, reducing potential bias but increasing variance. A wider bandwidth increases the sample size but may introduce confounding factors.
    • The choice of bandwidth can affect the estimated treatment effect. Often, the results should be checked for robustness by trying different bandwidths.
    • If the results are similar across different bandwidth choices, the estimate is more reliable.4. Placebo Tests and Robustness:
    • Placebo Tests: To check for manipulation or other sources of bias, you can conduct placebo tests by testing for discontinuities at values far from the cutoff where you wouldn’t expect any treatment effect. If you find a significant discontinuity in a place where no treatment is expected, it may indicate a problem with the validity of your RDD.
    • Robustness Checks: Check the robustness of the results by varying the specification of the model. For example, you could use different polynomial orders for the running variable, or estimate the model with and without control variables.5. Fuzzy RDD Interpretation:
    • If you are working with a fuzzy RDD, where the treatment is not perfectly determined by the cutoff (i.e., some units above the cutoff do not receive treatment and some below the cutoff do receive treatment), you must interpret the results as an intent-to-treat (ITT) effect, rather than a local treatment effect (LATE).
    • In this case, you would also need to estimate the local average treatment effect on the treated (LATT), which takes into account the proportion of individuals actually receiving the treatment above the cutoff.6. Statistical Significance:
    • P-value: The treatment effect should be evaluated for statistical significance, often using a p-value (e.g., p < 0.05). A p-value below 0.05 suggests that the treatment effect is unlikely to have occurred by chance, providing evidence that the treatment had an effect.
    • Confidence Intervals (CI): Confidence intervals provide a range of plausible values for the treatment effect. A narrow CI suggests a more precise estimate, while a wide CI suggests more uncertainty in the result.7. Manipulation of the Running Variable:
    • A key assumption of RDD is that the running variable (e.g., test score, income) cannot be manipulated around the cutoff. If individuals can manipulate the running variable to gain treatment, the design becomes invalid.
    • You can check for manipulation of the running variable by examining its distribution near the cutoff. If there’s a large bunching of observations just above or below the cutoff, it could suggest manipulation, which would invalidate the RDD results.Example of Interpreting RDD Results:Let’s say you are analyzing the effect of a scholarship program where students with test scores above 80 receive a scholarship.
    • Estimate: The RDD analysis estimates that students just above the cutoff score (80) score, on average, 5 points higher than students just below the cutoff.
    • Graph: The scatter plot shows a clear jump in test scores at the cutoff, suggesting a valid RDD.
    • Significance: The treatment effect is statistically significant (p-value < 0.05), so you can confidently say that receiving the scholarship increases test scores.
    • Placebo Test: You conduct a placebo test using a cutoff of 75, where no treatment is expected, and find no significant jump in test scores. This supports the validity of the original RDD.
    • Bandwidth: You try different bandwidths (e.g., 5, 10 points above and below the cutoff) and find that the estimated treatment effect remains stable.Summary of Key Steps:
    • Examine the treatment effect: This tells you the magnitude and direction of the causal effect.
    • Check for continuity at the cutoff: Use graphs and statistical tests to ensure that the running variable behaves smoothly at the cutoff.
    • Assess robustness: Check the stability of the results across different bandwidths and specifications.
    • Validate assumptions: Ensure that there is no manipulation of the running variable and that the RDD design is valid.

    4 what is causal discovery ▸

    Causal discovery is the process of identifying causal relationships from observational data by creating a causal graph that illustrates these relationships. This graph helps to identify variables that have a causal effect on outcomes, potential treatments, and hypotheses for further experimentation or causal inference. By analyzing patterns and dependencies among variables, causal discovery aims to uncover the underlying causal structures within a dataset, aiding us in understanding the true drivers behind observed phenomena and helping us make informed decisions based on data. In short, it is like being a detective, inferring how variables are causally related to one another.How can causal discovery help you?Causal discovery can help answer several important questions:

    • Which variables have a causal effect on a certain outcome? Example: Identifying that push notifications and the user interface design impact user engagement.
    • What are potential treatments or interventions? Example: Determining that improving loading speed could enhance user satisfaction.
    • What are the causal paths in the data? Example: Discovering that sending personalized push notifications leads to higher user engagement by increasing the likelihood that users will open the app.By answering these questions, causal discovery can enable businesses and researchers to understand the mechanisms driving observed effects, develop targeted interventions to improve outcomes, and formulate hypotheses for further experimentation or causal inference analysis.Methods used for causal discoverySeveral methods are commonly used in causal discovery, including:
    • Constraint-based methods: Techniques that use conditional independence tests to infer causal relationships. (Try out PC, FCI)
    • Score-based methods: Approaches that search for the best-fitting causal graph based on a scoring criterion. (Try out GES, KCRL)
    • Functional causal models (FCMs): Models that represent the effect variable as a function of its direct causes and some noise term. (Try out DirectLiNGAM, DECI)
    • Causal Discovery from Time-Series Data:
    • Leverage temporal ordering to infer causality (e.g., Granger Causality).
    • Machine Learning-Based Approaches:
    • Modern causal discovery methods incorporate deep learning, reinforcement learning, and Bayesian methods for scalability and flexibility.By leveraging these methods, we can uncover causal relationships and make data-driven decisions that lead to better outcomes.     5 What is causal inference ▸

    Causal inference is the process of estimating and understanding the causal effect of one variable on another. It focuses on quantifying and reasoning about cause-and-effect relationships, typically through observational data or experimental data.

    For example:

    • Does advertising (A) cause an increase in sales (B), and by how much?
    • What is the impact of a new medication (X) on patient recovery rates (Y)?Causal inference seeks to answer "what if" questions, such as:
    • Counterfactuals: What would have happened to sales if advertising had not been run?
    • Interventions: What happens to an outcome when a variable is actively manipulated?Steps in Causal Inference
    • Define the Causal Model:
    • Specify the causal relationships (often using Directed Acyclic Graphs, or DAGs).
    • Identify the Causal Effect:
    • Use statistical methods like matching, regression, or instrumental variables to isolate the causal impact.
    • Estimate the Causal Effect:
    • Employ models like difference-in-differences, propensity score matching, or A/B testing.
    • Evaluate Assumptions:
    • Causal inference relies on assumptions like no unmeasured confounding, correct model specification, and sufficient data.

    Causal inference is a methodology used to determine the effect of a treatment or intervention on an outcome. In causal inference, we often deal with various causal estimands (the quantity to be estimated), such as:

    • Average Treatment Effect (ATE): The average effect of a treatment across the entire population. ATE = E [ Y(1) — Y(0) ]
    • Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE): The average effect of a treatment for a specific subgroup within the population. CATE = E [ Y(1) — Y(0) | X = x ]
    • Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT): The average effect of a treatment on those who actually received the treatment. ATT = E [ Y(1) — Y(0) | T = 1 ]
    • Individual Treatment Effect (ITE): The effect of a treatment on an individual. ITE = Y(1)_i — Y(0)_iHere, Y(1) is the outcome under the treatment and Y(0) is the outcome without the treatment; X = x represents a population subgroup; T = 1 indicates treated population; and i represents an individual.Causal inference can help estimate the effect of a treatment on an outcome of interest.Let’s use a simple example to illustrate these concepts. Suppose we want to understand the effect of a new educational program on student performance. Ideally, we would need to know how the students perform with and without the program at the same time. Obviously, this isn’t possible in reality, but for the sake of understanding, let’s imagine that we have this data. Assume we know the location of the students as either A or B, and that students 1, 2, 3, and 4 eventually enroll in the new program. (This is just a fictional scenario to help us understand the estimands better.)Example data to help explain estimandsIn this example, we can see the performance outcomes for each student with and without the program. By comparing these outcomes, we can estimate the effect of the program.
    • ATE: The average difference in performance with and without the program across all students. The average performance without the program is 73.25 and with the program is 80.5, so the ATE is 7.25.
    • CATE: The average difference in performance for a specific subgroup, such as students in location A. The average performance without the program is 80.5 and with the program is 86, so the CATE is 5.5.
    • ATT: The average difference in performance for students who participated in the program. The average performance without the program is 71 and with the program is 80.75, so the ATT is 9.75.
    • ITE: The effect of the program on each individual student. For student 1, the performance with the program is 79 and without it is 77, so the ITE is 2.How can causal inference help you?Causal inference can help answer various business questions, such as:
    • How much did the new feature increase user engagement? (ATE)
    • What is the impact of a new targeted campaign on customer retention? (ATT)
    • How does the effect of a marketing campaign vary across different customer segments? (CATE)

Let’s explore two primary approaches within causal inference: Experimentation and non-experimentation methods.ExperimentationExperimentation is a method used to establish causal relationships by manipulating one or more variables and observing the effect on an outcome. It’s true that confounding factors can complicate things, but there are several methods that can help us get closer to understanding causality even without experiments. These methods are valuable when experimentation isn’t feasible. Let’s look at some broad categories of methods:

    Observational methods: These rely on data collected without manipulating the study environment. They aim to control for confounding factors to estimate causal effects and are particularly useful when conducting experiments is impractical or unethical.

    Quasi-experimental designs: These are used to identify causal relationships when random assignment to treatment and control groups is not feasible. They attempt to mimic the conditions of an experiment to estimate causal effects.

    Counterfactual analysis: This involves comparing the observed outcome with the hypothetical outcome that would have occurred if the treatment had not been applied. This approach is based on the potential outcomes framework.

    Here are some commonly used causal inference techniques that fall under the categories of observational methods, quasi-experimental designs, and counterfactual analysis. Because some of these methods can fall under multiple categories, we list them below:

    • Propensity Score Matching (PSM): Pairs individuals who received the treatment with those who didn’t, based on similar characteristics, to reduce bias from confounding variables.
    • Instrumental Variables (IV): Uses variables related to the treatment but not directly to the outcome (except through the treatment) to isolate the causal effect by providing a source of variation in the treatment that isn’t influenced by confounders.
    • Double Machine Learning (DML): Utilizes Machine Learning to control for confounders by creating “clean” versions of treatment and outcome variables, by subtracting estimated effects from confounders, providing more accurate causal estimates.
    • Difference-in-Differences (DiD): Compares the changes in outcomes over time between a treatment group and a control group, helping to control for confounding variables that affect both groups similarly over time.
    • Synthetic Control Method (SC): Constructs a synthetic control group by combining data from multiple untreated units to estimate what would have happened to the treated unit in the absence of the treatment.
    • Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS): Uses Bayesian methods to model time series data. The effect of an intervention is inferred by comparing observed data with a counterfactual scenario, estimating what would have happened without the intervention.
    • Regression Discontinuity Design (RDD): Uses a cutoff or threshold to assign treatment. By comparing individuals just above and below this cutoff, RDD estimates the causal effect, assuming those near the threshold are similar except for the treatment.These methods offer alternatives when experimentation isn’t feasible, enabling researchers to estimate causal effects even in the presence of confounding factors.

    7 what is propensity score matching(PSM) ▸

    PSM involves the calculation of the propensity score, which is the probability of a unit (e.g., a person, school, hospital) receiving a particular treatment given a set of observed characteristics. The propensity score is calculated using logistic regression or other statistical methods. Once the propensity scores are calculated, units in the treatment and control groups are matched based on these scores. This process aims to create a synthetic control group that is statistically similar to the treatment group in terms of the observed covariates. PSM is used in observational studies where random assignment to a treatment and control group is not feasible, and it helps in reducing selection bias by equating groups based on these covariates.

    In Plain English:Imagine you want to know if a training program helps employees get promoted faster. However, the people who choose to attend the training program may already be different in some way – maybe they are more motivated, more experienced, or have better skills. This makes it hard to tell whether the training itself helped, or if those differences caused the promotions.Propensity Score Matching (PSM) helps solve this problem by matching people in the training group with similar people in the non-training group based on their characteristics (like motivation, experience, skills, etc.). After matching, you can compare the two groups as if they were more alike, helping you estimate the true effect of the training program.Example:Problem:You have data on 100 employees, where:

    • 50 attended the training (treatment group).
    • 50 did not attend (control group).Each employee has characteristics like:
    • Years of experience.
    • Education level.
    • Motivation score (on a scale of 1-10).Step 1: Calculate Propensity ScoresYou create a model (e.g., logistic regression) to calculate the probability (propensity) of each employee attending the training based on their characteristics. For example:
    • Employee A: 80% chance of attending training (high motivation, lots of experience).
    • Employee B: 20% chance of attending training (low motivation, less experience).Step 2: Match EmployeesFind a "match" for each person in the training group from the control group who has a similar propensity score. For example:
    • Employee A (training group, 80% chance) is matched with Employee C (control group, 78% chance).
    • Employee B (training group, 20% chance) is matched with Employee D (control group, 22% chance).Step 3: Compare OutcomesNow you compare the promotion rates of the matched pairs. For example:
    • Out of 50 matched pairs, 30 people in the training group were promoted, while only 20 people in the control group were promoted.
    • This suggests that the training program may have increased the likelihood of promotion.Why Use PSM?Without PSM, you’d be comparing apples to oranges because the training group and control group might differ in ways unrelated to the training itself. PSM helps make a fair comparison by ensuring you’re comparing people who are as similar as possible, except for whether or not they attended the training.

    • Reducing Selection Bias: In studies where the treatment group is not randomly selected, PSM helps in balancing the groups based on observed covariates, reducing the bias due to non-random assignment.

    • Example:
    • You want to study the effect of smoking on lung cancer, but you cannot ethically assign people to start smoking. Instead, you rely on observational data where some people are smokers and others are not.2. When There is Selection Bias in Observational Data
    • In observational studies, people who receive the treatment may be systematically different from those who don't (e.g., more motivated, younger, healthier, etc.). This creates selection bias.
    • PSM accounts for these differences by matching individuals in the treatment and control groups based on their likelihood (propensity) of receiving the treatment.3. When You Have Measurable Covariates
    • PSM requires data on observable characteristics (covariates) that influence the likelihood of receiving the treatment.
    • Example:
    • You have data on age, education, and income for people who attended a training program. PSM uses these characteristics to create comparable treatment and control groups.4. When You Need a Fair Comparison
    • PSM is used to mimic the conditions of a randomized trial by creating a pseudo-randomized control group that is as similar as possible to the treatment group.
    • Example:
    • If wealthier people are more likely to use a health app, PSM can match app users with non-users of similar wealth, reducing bias.5. When You Want to Estimate Causal Effects
    • PSM is often used to estimate the average treatment effect on the treated (ATT), which measures the effect of the treatment for individuals who actually received it.
    • Example:
    • You want to know if a job training program improves employment outcomes for those who attended. PSM can help estimate the program's causal impact by comparing attendees with non-attendees who are similar in their observed characteristics.Examples of ApplicationsHealthcare:
    • Evaluating the effect of a new drug on patient recovery rates when clinical trials were not conducted.
    • Example: Comparing patients who received a new drug to those who did not, while matching based on age, gender, and pre-existing conditions.Policy Analysis:
    • Assessing the impact of a policy or intervention.
    • Example: Estimating the effect of free school lunches on student performance by comparing students who received free lunches with similar students who did not.Economics:
    • Measuring the impact of education or training programs on earnings.
    • Example: Matching individuals who attended a job training program with similar individuals who did not (based on age, education, and prior work experience).Marketing:
    • Estimating the effectiveness of a marketing campaign.
    • Example: Comparing customers who saw an ad with those who didn’t, matching on characteristics like purchase history and demographics.Social Sciences:
    • Studying the impact of behaviors or lifestyle choices.

    • When Key Covariates Are Missing:
    • PSM only accounts for observable covariates. If important factors are unobserved or unmeasured, the results can still be biased.
    • When There is Insufficient Overlap (Common Support):
    • If the treatment and control groups are very different (e.g., no individuals in the control group with similar propensity scores to the treatment group), PSM cannot create good matches.
    • When Sample Size is Too Small:
    • PSM requires a large enough sample to find good matches. Small datasets may not allow for meaningful matching.

    18 What is Difference-in-Differences (DiD)? ▸

    Difference-in-Differences (DiD)Difference-in-Differences (DiD) is a quasi-experimental method used to estimate causal effects by comparing the changes in outcomes over time between a treatment group and a control group. The key idea is to measure the difference in trends before and after a treatment (or intervention), controlling for baseline differences between groups.

    DiD relies on comparing the changes in outcomes over time between a group that is exposed to some treatment or intervention (the treatment group) and a group that is not (the control group).If the treatment has no effect on the subjects, you would deem a constant difference in the trends between the treatment and control groups. Otherwise, the trends will change in the treatment group during the intervention period given the intervention effect.

    Key Assumptions

    • Parallel Trends Assumption:
    • In the absence of the treatment, the treatment group and control group would have followed the same trend over time.
    • If this assumption is violated, the estimated treatment effect may be biased.
    • No Simultaneous Confounding Events:
    • No other factors or interventions should disproportionately affect either group during the study period.
    • Consistency in Measurement:
    • The outcome variable should be measured consistently across time and groups.Difference in Differences (DiD) is a statistical analysis that measures the change in the difference between the control and treatment groups over the course of pre and post-intervention periods. DiD is often applied on a dataset that is (1) aggregated at a group level (e.g. geography, market) and (2) tracked across a time horizon with daily, weekly, or monthly granularity.

    In general, you use DiD when subjects of an experiment cannot be randomly assigned to the control and treatment groups.

    The basic equation for a DiD analysis is:ΔY= ΔY_Treated − ΔY_ControlWhere:

    • ΔY represents the estimated treatment effect or the intervention effect.
    • ΔY_Treatment is the difference in the outcome variable for the treatment group from before and after the treatment is applied (we call these pre-and post-intervention periods)
    • ΔY_Control is the difference in the outcome variable for the control group over the same periods as the treatment.

    Intuitively, you can interpret this as follows. Assume that subjects in the treatment and control groups are exactly the same, and you observe their behavior over time. If the control saw a positive delta of let’s say 100, you should observe exactly the same delta for the treatment. But, if you observe that the delta in the treatment is 120, the additional 20 increase is most likely due to the treatment having an effect on the subjects in the treatment group during the intervention period. And, that “difference” in the “differences” is the main essence of how the DiD method measures the causal effect of a treatment。

    Synthetic ControlsSynthetic Control Method (SCM) is a statistical technique used in causal inference to evaluate the effect of an intervention (or treatment) when randomization is not feasible. It creates a synthetic control group by combining multiple untreated units (or groups) into a weighted average that closely matches the pre-treatment characteristics of the treated unit.The synthetic control acts as a counterfactual: it estimates what would have happened to the treated unit if the treatment had not occurred.How Does it Work?

    • Select a Treated Unit:
    • Identify the unit (e.g., a region, organization, or individual) that receives the treatment.
    • Choose Potential Control Units:
    • Select untreated units that are similar to the treated unit in terms of pre-treatment characteristics.
    • Construct a Synthetic Control:
    • Use a weighted combination of the control units to create a synthetic unit that mimics the treated unit's pre-treatment outcomes.
    • Compare Outcomes:
    • After the treatment, compare the outcomes of the treated unit to the synthetic control.
    • The difference represents the estimated causal effect of the treatment.Key Assumptions
    • Pre-Treatment Similarity:
    • The synthetic control closely matches the treated unit’s outcomes in the pre-treatment period.
    • No Spillover Effects:
    • The treatment does not indirectly affect control units.
    • Exogeneity:
    • There are no unmeasured confounders that influence both the treatment and the outcome.ExampleScenario:A state raises its minimum wage, and you want to evaluate its impact on employment. You construct a synthetic control by combining employment data from similar states that did not raise the minimum wage.
    • Treated Unit:
    • The state that increased its minimum wage.
    • Control Units:
    • Other states with similar pre-treatment employment trends.
    • Synthetic Control:
    • A weighted combination of control states to mimic the treated state's pre-treatment employment.
    • Outcome Comparison:
    • Compare the treated state's employment trends after the wage increase to the synthetic control’s trends.
    • The difference is the estimated causal effect of the minimum wage increase.Advantages
    • Causal Inference Without Randomization:
    • Useful in observational studies where randomization is impossible.
    • Customizable Controls:
    • The synthetic control can better approximate the treated unit than any single control unit.
    • Visual and Intuitive:
    • The method provides clear comparisons of trends pre- and post-treatment.Limitations
    • Requires High-Quality Data:
    • The method relies on sufficient pre-treatment data and well-matched controls.
    • Only One Treated Unit:
    • Designed for cases with a single treated unit (or small number of treated units).
    • Subjectivity in Control Selection:
    • Selecting the pool of potential controls can introduce bias.
    • Local Validity:
    • Estimates are valid for the treated unit but may not generalize to other contexts.Applications
    • Policy Evaluation:
    • Example: Evaluating the impact of a tax policy in one country by comparing it to a synthetic country constructed from others.
    • Public Health:
    • Example: Assessing the effect of a smoking ban on hospitalization rates.

    Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS)Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS) is a statistical modeling framework that uses Bayesian methods to analyze and forecast time series data. It is particularly powerful for:

    • Modeling time series with structural components (e.g., trend, seasonality).
    • Handling causal inference problems, such as evaluating the effect of an intervention.
    • Providing probabilistic forecasts with uncertainty intervals.Key Features of BSTS
    • Structural Components:
    • BSTS decomposes a time series into interpretable components, such as:
    • Trend: Long-term growth or decline.
    • Seasonality: Repeated patterns at regular intervals (e.g., weekly or yearly cycles).
    • Regressors: Explanatory variables affecting the time series.
    • Bayesian Framework:
    • Parameters are estimated using Bayesian inference, which incorporates prior beliefs and updates them based on observed data.
    • This provides a distribution of possible outcomes rather than a single point estimate.
    • Dynamic Components:
    • Components can evolve over time, allowing the model to adapt to changes in the data.
    • Intervention Analysis:
    • BSTS is often used for causal impact analysis, evaluating how an intervention (e.g., a policy or marketing campaign) affects the outcome.How Does BSTS Work?
    • Bayesian Inference:
    • Priors are specified for the model parameters.
    • The posterior distribution is estimated using techniques like Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC).
    • Prediction:
    • Forecasts are made by sampling from the posterior distribution, which provides uncertainty intervals.
    • Intervention Analysis:
    • Compare the observed time series to the model's predicted counterfactual (what would have happened without the intervention).Use Cases for BSTS
    • Causal Impact Analysis:
    • Evaluate the effect of an intervention (e.g., a new policy, marketing campaign) by comparing actual outcomes to a counterfactual prediction.
    • Example: Google’s CausalImpact library is built on BSTS for this purpose.
    • Time Series Forecasting:
    • Make probabilistic forecasts with confidence intervals.
    • Example: Predicting future sales, website traffic, or stock prices.
    • Seasonal and Trend Decomposition:
    • Understand the underlying patterns in the data by isolating trend and seasonal effects.
    • Outlier Detection:
    • Identify anomalies by comparing observed values to model predictions.Advantages of BSTS
    • Flexible and Interpretable:
    • Models structural components (trend, seasonality) separately, making it easy to understand.
    • Handles Missing Data:
    • Bayesian methods can naturally handle missing observations.
    • Uncertainty Quantification:
    • Provides probabilistic predictions with uncertainty intervals.
    • Dynamic Modeling:
    • Can adapt to changes in trends or seasonality over time.Limitations of BSTS
    • Computationally Intensive:
    • Bayesian methods (e.g., MCMC) can be slow, especially for large datasets.
    • Requires Expertise:
    • Setting up priors and interpreting posterior distributions requires statistical knowledge.
    • Complexity:
    • May be overkill for simple time series tasks.Example: Causal Impact AnalysisSuppose a company runs a marketing campaign and wants to measure its impact on website traffic. The time series consists of daily website visits before and after the campaign.

    • A higher-level model that takes the outputs of the base learners as input and produces the final prediction.

    29 Meta learner limition ▸

    3. Limitations of Meta LearnersMeta-learners, while powerful tools in causal inference, have several limitations that are important to consider:

    • Quality of Observational Data: The effectiveness of meta-learners is highly dependent on the quality of the observational data used. Issues like missing data, measurement errors, or unmeasured confounders can significantly impact the results.
    • Model Specification: The performance of meta-learners depends heavily on the correct specification of the underlying models. Incorrect model specification can lead to biased estimates of treatment effects.
    • Computational Complexity: Some meta-learner approaches, especially those involving complex models or large datasets, can be computationally intensive, requiring significant resources and time for analysis.
    • Sensitivity to Overfitting: Meta-learners that use machine learning models are prone to overfitting, especially in scenarios with a large number of covariates relative to the number of observations.
    • Requirement for Large Sample Sizes: Accurate estimation of heterogeneous treatment effects often requires large sample sizes, particularly when analyzing subgroups within the data.
