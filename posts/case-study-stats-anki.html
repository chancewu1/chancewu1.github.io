    1 what are Clustering Survival analysis ▸

    Clustering Survival Analysis

    This combines clustering and survival analysis to group similar individuals (or subjects) based on patterns in their survival data.

    • Survival Analysis: Statistical methods to estimate the time until an event occurs (e.g., failure of a machine, a medical condition). It deals with censored data, where the event may not have occurred for some subjects.
    • Clustering: Groups data points with similar features into clusters (e.g., k-means, hierarchical clustering).

    Example: Healthcare

    • Scenario: A hospital wants to predict survival rates for cancer patients.
    • Data: Patient features like age, tumor size, treatment type, and whether the patient survived or their survival time (censored data if they’re still alive).
    • How it works:
    • Clustering:
    • Group patients with similar features (e.g., younger patients with smaller tumors might form one cluster, older patients with larger tumors might form another).
    • Survival Analysis:
    • For each cluster, apply survival analysis methods like the Kaplan-Meier estimator to model the survival curves of the group.
    • Outcome:
    • The hospital finds that Cluster A (young, small tumors) has an 85% survival rate at 5 years, while Cluster B (older, large tumors) has only 40%.
    2 Challenging a design ▸

    1. Selection Bias

    Definition: Occurs when the sample is not representative of the population due to improper sampling methods.

    Example:

    • A clinical trial only includes participants under 50, but the drug is meant for all age groups.

    Solution:

    • Use random sampling to ensure all individuals have an equal chance of being selected.
    • Stratify the sample based on key population characteristics.

    2. Ordering Bias

    Definition: Occurs when the order of questions or options affects responses.

    Example:

    • A survey asks, “What do you think about this excellent new product?” The positive wording might bias answers.

    Solution:

    • Randomize the order of questions or options.
    • Use neutral, unbiased wording for survey questions.

    3. Non-Response Bias

    Definition: Happens when individuals who don’t respond differ systematically from those who do.

    Example:

    • A survey on household income gets low response rates from high-income individuals, skewing the results.

    Solution:

    • Follow up with non-respondents to increase response rates.
    • Use weighting adjustments to account for the missing data.

    4. Sampling Bias

    Definition: Occurs when the sample is not randomly chosen, leading to under- or over-representation of certain groups.

    Example:

    • Conducting a poll only in urban areas to gauge national opinions.

    Solution:

    • Use probability sampling (e.g., stratified or cluster sampling).
    • Ensure sample coverage across key demographic factors.

    5. Reporting Bias

    Definition: Happens when results or observations are selectively reported, ignoring contradictory findings.

    Example:

    • A study on drug effectiveness publishes only the trials with positive results, hiding failed trials.

    Solution:

    • Encourage pre-registration of studies with defined outcomes.
    • Publish all results, even negative or inconclusive ones.

    6. Self-Selection Bias

    Definition: Arises when individuals decide whether to participate, and those who do are not representative of the population.

    Example:

    • An online survey about mental health may attract only individuals struggling with mental health issues.

    Solution:

    • Use randomized invitations to ensure everyone has a chance to participate.
    • Incentivize participation across diverse groups.

    7. Survivor Bias

    Definition: Focuses only on individuals or data points that survived a selection process, ignoring those that didn’t.

    Example:

    • Analyzing only successful businesses to find traits of success, ignoring failed businesses.

    Solution:

    • Include data on entities or individuals that didn’t “survive” the process.
    • Balance analyses to account for missing data.

    8. Confounding Variables

    Definition: When an unmeasured variable influences both the independent and dependent variables.

    Example:

    • A study shows coffee drinkers live longer, but neglects to account for exercise habits (a confounder).

    Solution:

    • Use randomized controlled trials (RCTs) to eliminate confounders.
    • Apply statistical methods like multiple regression or propensity score matching to control for confounders.

    9. Regression to the Mean

    Definition: Extreme measurements tend to move closer to the mean in subsequent observations.

    Example:

    • A group of students performs exceptionally poorly on one test and improves significantly on the next, but this improvement might just be regression to the mean.

    Solution:

    • Include a control group to distinguish true effects from statistical fluctuations.
    • Collect multiple measurements over time.

    10. Problem with Triggering

    Definition: The process of data collection influences behavior or results.

    Example:

    • People behave differently when they know they are being observed, such as in a study about workplace productivity.

    Solution:

    • Use blinded experiments where participants don’t know they’re being studied.
    • Conduct naturalistic observations when possible.

    11. Recall Bias

    Definition: Occurs when participants fail to remember past events accurately.

    Example:

    • Patients asked to recall their diet from the past year may provide inaccurate information.

    Solution:

    • Use objective data (e.g., food logs) when possible.
    • Shorten the recall period (e.g., ask about the past week instead of the past year).

    12. Anchoring Bias

    Definition: Initial information influences subsequent judgments or responses.

    Example:

    • When estimating the price of a house, people anchor on the listed price even if it’s artificially high.

    Solution:

    • Present data or information without any leading or suggestive references.
    • Allow participants to make decisions independently.

    General Strategies to Solve Bias:

    • Randomization:
    • Randomly select participants, variables, or treatments to reduce selection bias and confounding.
    • Blinding:
    • Use single or double-blinding to prevent influencing participant or researcher behavior.
    • Balanced Data Collection:
    • Ensure data represents all subgroups in the population.
    • Cross-Validation:
    • Validate results across different datasets to ensure robustness.
    • Pre-Registration:
    • Define study methods and outcomes before conducting research to prevent selective reporting.
    3 what is History Effect ▸

    History Effects in Research

    Definition:

    History effects refer to the influence of external events or occurrences that happen during the course of a study and affect the participants' outcomes, independent of the study's intervention. These effects can distort the results of a study, making it difficult to determine whether the observed outcomes are due to the intervention or external factors.

    When Do History Effects Occur?

    • They occur in longitudinal studies or experiments that span a significant amount of time.
    • They are especially problematic in quasi-experiments, where researchers cannot fully control for external factors.

    Examples of History Effects

    • Educational Study Example:
    • A study measures the impact of a new teaching method on students' test scores over a school year.
    • During the year, a nationwide teacher strike disrupts classroom learning, impacting test scores. The strike (a historical event) influences the results, not the teaching method.
    • Health Study Example:
    • A study assesses the effectiveness of a fitness program in reducing weight over six months.
    • During the study, a new popular diet trend emerges, and participants independently adopt it. The observed weight loss may be due to the diet, not the fitness program.
    • Economic Study Example:
    • A company introduces a new productivity tool and measures employee performance over a year.
    • During the year, an economic downturn affects the industry, leading to layoffs and low morale. The downturn impacts productivity independently of the tool.

    Impact of History Effects

    • Threat to Internal Validity:
    • History effects make it difficult to attribute changes in the dependent variable to the independent variable.
    • Confounding Variables:
    • The historical event becomes a confounder, obscuring the true relationship between variables.

    How to Minimize or Control History Effects

    • Control Group:
    • Use a control group that is not exposed to the intervention. If both the control and experimental groups are affected by the historical event, its impact can be isolated.
    • Example: If both groups are affected by a teacher strike, the impact on test scores can be accounted for.
    • Shorten Study Duration:
    • Reduce the duration of the study to minimize the chance of external events affecting results.
    • Randomization:
    • Randomly assign participants to groups to ensure that the effect of external events is evenly distributed.
    • Include Pre- and Post-Test Measurements:
    • Measure outcomes before and after the intervention. Compare any external event’s potential impact on the pre-test and post-test differences.
    • Monitor for Historical Events:
    • Track major external events during the study period and account for their potential influence in the analysis (e.g., use statistical methods to adjust for external events).
    • Multisite Studies:
    • Conduct the study in multiple locations. If a historical event occurs in one area, its effects can be compared to unaffected areas.

    Example of Controlling History Effects

    Scenario:

    A company tests a new training program to improve employee productivity over six months.

    • Potential History Effect: During the study, a global event (e.g., a pandemic or a change in company leadership) could affect employee productivity.
    • Control:
    • Include a group of employees who do not undergo the training (control group).
    • If productivity drops in both groups due to the event, you can adjust your conclusions to separate the historical event's impact from the training program's effect.

    Takeaway

    History effects are a common challenge in research, especially when the study spans a long time. Using robust experimental designs (e.g., control groups, randomization) and monitoring external events can help mitigate their impact and ensure valid results.

    4 Testing effect ▸

    Testing Effect

    The testing effect (or practice effect) refers to the phenomenon where the act of taking a test or being exposed to assessment materials influences subsequent performance on the same or similar tests. This happens because repeated testing improves memory recall or understanding of the material, regardless of any interventions or treatments applied.

    Key Characteristics

    • The testing effect occurs because repeated exposure to test material leads to better retention and learning, rather than reflecting the effectiveness of an intervention.
    • It can threaten the internal validity of an experiment, especially in studies with pre-tests and post-tests.

    When Does the Testing Effect Occur?

    • Repeated Testing:
    • Participants are exposed to the same or similar tests multiple times.
    • Memory Recall:
    • The test itself reinforces learning or memory recall, improving performance in subsequent tests.

    Examples of the Testing Effect

    • Educational Research:
    • A study evaluates a new teaching method by giving students a pre-test and a post-test.
    • The post-test scores improve, but not because of the teaching method—it’s because the students already practiced answering similar questions in the pre-test.
    • Cognitive Psychology Experiment:
    • Participants are given a list of words to recall, followed by repeated memory tests over time.
    • Their recall improves, not because of enhanced cognitive strategies but due to repeated exposure to the same material.
    • Employee Training Program:
    • A company evaluates a training program by administering a pre-training and post-training test.
    • Employees perform better on the post-training test because they already encountered the questions during the pre-training test, not because of the training program itself.

    Impact of the Testing Effect

    • Threat to Internal Validity:
    • It becomes difficult to determine whether performance improvements are due to the intervention or the testing effect.
    • Bias in Results:
    • Results may falsely show an effect of the intervention when the improvement is actually due to repeated exposure to the test.

    How to Minimize or Control the Testing Effect

    • Control Group:
    • Include a group that does not take the pre-test to compare their performance with the group that did. This helps isolate the testing effect.
    • Alternate Test Forms:
    • Use different versions of the test for the pre-test and post-test to reduce familiarity with specific questions.
    • Delay Between Tests:
    • Increase the time interval between the pre-test and post-test to reduce the likelihood that participants remember the test content.
    • Counterbalancing:
    • Randomly assign participants to take tests in different orders or formats to account for any systematic effects of repeated testing.
    • Use Post-Test Only:
    • In some cases, skip the pre-test entirely and compare the intervention group to a control group with only a post-test.
    • Measure Learning Gains:
    • If the testing effect is inevitable, measure the improvement between tests as a baseline and compare it to gains after the intervention.

    Example of Controlling the Testing Effect

    Scenario:

    A school wants to measure the impact of a new math teaching method.

    • Issue: Students take a math pre-test and then a post-test after the intervention. The testing effect might improve their scores simply because they practiced the test questions in the pre-test.
    • Solution:
    • Use two groups:
    • Group A: Takes the pre-test and post-test (experimental group).
    • Group B: Takes only the post-test (control group).
    • Use different but equivalent versions of the test (e.g., different math problems).
    • Compare Group B's post-test performance to Group A's to separate the effects of the intervention from the testing effect.

    Takeaway

    The testing effect is an important phenomenon to consider in research. While it can be a natural part of the learning process, it can confound results if not properly controlled. Using control groups, alternate test forms, and careful experimental designs can help mitigate its impact.

    5 Regression to the Mean ▸

    Definition:

    Regression to the mean is a statistical phenomenon that occurs when extreme measurements (very high or very low values) on one occasion tend to move closer to the average (mean) on subsequent measurements, purely due to random variability.

    Why Does Regression to the Mean Happen?

    • Random Variation: Extreme values often occur due to a combination of true effects and random noise.
    • Statistical Tendency: Over repeated measurements, random noise balances out, causing extreme values to move closer to the mean.

    Examples of Regression to the Mean

    1. Sports Performance:

    • Scenario: A basketball player has an incredible game, scoring well above their average (e.g., 50 points). In the next game, their score is closer to their usual average (e.g., 25 points).
    • Reason: The exceptional performance may be partly due to random factors (e.g., weak defense or lucky shots), and the next game reflects their typical ability.

    2. Medical Studies:

    • Scenario: A group of patients with very high blood pressure is studied. After an intervention, their blood pressure decreases.
    • Reason: Some of the initial high readings were due to random fluctuations (e.g., stress during the first measurement), and their subsequent readings move closer to the average even without treatment.

    3. Education:

    • Scenario: A teacher gives a particularly difficult test, and the class scores poorly. The next test shows improved scores.
    • Reason: The initial low scores were partly due to the unusually hard test, and the next test reflects typical performance.

    Impact of Regression to the Mean

    • Misinterpretation of Interventions:
    • Improvements (or declines) may be incorrectly attributed to an intervention, even though they are due to regression to the mean.
    • Example: After a weight loss program, participants with extreme initial weights may show reductions simply because of this phenomenon.
    • Threat to Internal Validity:
    • If not accounted for, regression to the mean can confound the results of studies and experiments.

    How to Detect and Address Regression to the Mean

    • Use a Control Group:
    • Include a group that does not receive the intervention.
    • If both groups show similar changes, regression to the mean is likely at play.
    • Randomize Assignment:
    • Randomly assign participants to intervention and control groups to balance out extreme values.
    • Measure Multiple Baselines:
    • Take several pre-treatment measurements to reduce the influence of extreme initial values.
    • Adjust for Initial Values:
    • Use statistical methods like ANCOVA (Analysis of Covariance) to adjust for baseline differences.
    • Be Cautious with Extreme Groups:
    • Avoid selecting participants solely based on extreme values. For example:
    • Instead of selecting "only students with the lowest scores," consider a broader range.

    Example of Controlling for Regression to the Mean

    Scenario: Evaluating a Fitness Program

    • Setup: A study is conducted to test the effectiveness of a fitness program. Participants are selected based on their extreme low fitness scores.
    • Problem: Their improvement might be due to regression to the mean, not the program.
    • Solution:
    • Include a control group with low fitness scores that doesn’t participate in the program.
    • Compare changes in fitness between the intervention group and the control group.
    • If both groups improve similarly, the changes are likely due to regression to the mean, not the fitness program.

    Takeaway

    Regression to the mean is a natural statistical effect that can lead to misinterpretation of results in studies and experiments. By using proper experimental designs, control groups, and statistical adjustments, researchers can account for this phenomenon and draw more accurate conclusions.

    • Regression to the mean occurs when extreme values in a baseline measurement tend to move closer to the mean during follow-up due to random variation. This can falsely suggest a treatment effect when one doesn’t exist.
    • In experiments, if groups have differences in baseline measurements, observed outcomes may be confounded by these baseline differences.2. How ANCOVA Adjusts for Baseline DifferencesANCOVA incorporates a covariate (e.g., baseline measure) into the analysis to control for its influence on the dependent variable. Here's how:
    • The model assumes the dependent variable (e.g., post-intervention scores) is influenced by both:
    • The treatment or intervention.
    • Baseline values (covariate).
    • By adjusting for baseline values, ANCOVA removes their impact from the post-intervention outcomes. This isolates the treatment effect and controls for any differences that existed prior to the intervention.3. Reducing Regression to the Mean
    • Regression to the mean happens because extreme baseline values are often due to random error, and future measurements naturally gravitate closer to the average.
    • By statistically adjusting for baseline scores, ANCOVA accounts for this phenomenon:
    • It models the relationship between baseline and outcome scores.
    • It separates true treatment effects from random variation at baseline.This ensures that the observed treatment effect is not simply a result of regression to the mean, but rather the actual effect of the intervention.4. Practical ExampleImagine an experiment testing a weight-loss program. Two groups (intervention and control) start with different baseline weights:
    • People in the intervention group might start with higher weights due to random selection.
    • Without adjustment, a reduction in weight might seem more significant in this group simply due to regression to the mean.Using ANCOVA:
    • Baseline weight is included as a covariate.
    • The analysis adjusts post-intervention weights based on initial baseline differences.
    • This ensures the comparison of weight loss is not biased by baseline differences or regression to the mean.5. Assumptions of ANCOVATo correctly remove the effects of regression to the mean:
    • The relationship between baseline and outcome scores (covariate and dependent variable) should be linear.
    • The covariate should be measured without error and unaffected by the intervention.
    • The groups should have similar slopes (homogeneity of regression assumption).6. Limitations
    • If the covariate is measured with high error, ANCOVA adjustments may be unreliable.

It provides a statistically sound way to account for variability in experiments and enhances the validity of causal inferences.

    7 handle positive skewed data with many zeros ▸

    Imagine a scenario where the data has a high proportion of zeros and exhibits a strong positive skew. What statistical test would you recommend to analyze the differences between two groups with this type of data distribution?

    • Log-Transformed, T-Test - This can resolve the skewness as the log could make the distribution normal. But, this may not resolve the inflation at zero.
    • Zero-Inflated Model - This is a two-step approach that involves binary classification model like the logistic regression that predicts 0 or non-zero, then a OLS model that predicts the continuous range, which are transformed with log.
    • Mann-Whitney U Test - This is most likely the most favorable test given the underlying condition. It’s a non-parametric test that does not make any assumption about the underlying shape of the distributions.

    Recommended Statistical Tests

    1. Mann-Whitney U Test (Non-parametric)

    • When to Use: If you are comparing the distributions of two independent groups.
    • Why: It does not assume normality and works well with skewed data.
    • Limitation: It does not account for the excess zeros directly; it only considers the ranks of the data.

    2. Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test (Non-parametric)

    • When to Use: If your data is paired or dependent (e.g., pre-test vs. post-test within the same group).
    • Why: It also avoids normality assumptions and can handle skewed data.

    3. Zero-Inflated Models

    • When to Use: If the zeros are due to a structural reason (e.g., participants cannot score above zero under certain conditions) or if the data exhibits overdispersion caused by zeros.
    • Model Options:
    • Zero-Inflated Poisson (ZIP): For count data with excess zeros.
    • Zero-Inflated Negative Binomial (ZINB): For overdispersed count data with excess zeros.
    • Why: These models explicitly separate the process generating the zeros from the process generating the positive values.

    4. Two-Part (Hurdle) Models

    • When to Use: If you believe there are two separate processes:
    • One determining whether the observation is zero or non-zero.
    • Another determining the value of the non-zero observations.
    • How It Works:
    • First part: Logistic regression (or similar) to model the presence of zeros.
    • Second part: A model for the non-zero values (e.g., log-transformed regression, gamma regression).
    • Why: It treats zero-inflation explicitly and models the continuous portion separately.

    5. Transformations + Parametric Tests

    • When to Use: If the proportion of zeros is not extreme, you can transform the data (e.g., log transformation, square root transformation) and then apply parametric tests like a t-test or ANOVA.
    • Why: Transformations can reduce skewness and make the data closer to normality.
    • Limitation: Zeros cannot be transformed with a log transformation (often handled by adding a small constant like 1 to all values).

    6. Permutation Tests

    • When to Use: If you want a robust method to compare groups without relying on parametric assumptions.
    • Why: Permutation tests work directly with the observed data and do not rely on specific distributional assumptions.
    • Example: Shuffle group labels many times to create a null distribution and compare the observed difference to this distribution.

    Which Test to Choose?

    • Small Proportion of Zeros: Consider transformations (e.g., log or square root) and parametric tests like a t-test or ANOVA.
    • High Proportion of Zeros:
    • If zeros are structural: Use zero-inflated models or two-part models.
    • If zeros are incidental: Use a non-parametric test (e.g., Mann-Whitney U) or permutation tests.
    • Strong Skew Without Excess Zeros: Use non-parametric tests.

    Example Scenario

    Data: Comparing the number of defective items produced by two factories. Many observations are zero (factories often have no defects), and the data is positively skewed.

    Recommendation:

    • Use a Zero-Inflated Poisson model if the data represents count values (e.g., number of defects).
    • If you don't have expertise in zero-inflated modeling, start with a Mann-Whitney U Test as a simpler alternative.

    8 what is non-parametric tests. ▸

    Non-Parametric Tests: What They Are and When to Use ThemNon-parametric tests are statistical methods that do not make assumptions about the underlying data distribution (e.g., normality). They are particularly useful when the data violate assumptions required by parametric tests or when the data are ordinal, ranked, or have outliers.Key Features of Non-Parametric Tests

    • No assumption of normality: Unlike parametric tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA), non-parametric tests do not assume that the data are normally distributed.
    • Robust to outliers and skewness: Non-parametric tests handle non-normal distributions, extreme values, or small sample sizes better than parametric tests.
    • Data type flexibility: Suitable for ordinal, ranked, or categorical data (not just interval/ratio).
    • Compare medians instead of means: Non-parametric tests often compare medians rather than means, making them more robust to skewed data.When to Use Non-Parametric Tests
    • Violation of normality: If your data are not normally distributed and cannot be transformed to approximate normality.
    • Small sample size: Non-parametric tests are better suited when the sample size is too small to reliably assess normality.
    • Outliers: If the data contain significant outliers that cannot be removed or transformed.
    • Ordinal or ranked data: When data are ordinal (e.g., Likert scales) or when you’re working with rankings instead of exact measurements.Common Non-Parametric TestsHere are some widely used non-parametric tests and their parametric counterparts:1. Tests for Comparing Two Groups
    • Parametric counterpart: Independent samples t-test
    • Non-parametric equivalent: Mann-Whitney U Test (or Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test)
    • Compares two independent groups based on ranks.
    • Example: Comparing test scores between two classes when scores are non-normal.
    • Parametric counterpart: Paired samples t-test
    • Non-parametric equivalent: Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test
    • Compares two related samples (e.g., before-and-after measurements) using ranks.2. Tests for Comparing Multiple Groups
    • Parametric counterpart: ANOVA
    • Non-parametric equivalent: Kruskal-Wallis Test
    • Tests differences in medians across three or more independent groups.
    • Example: Comparing customer satisfaction scores across multiple stores.
    • Parametric counterpart: Repeated Measures ANOVA
    • Non-parametric equivalent: Friedman Test
    • Compares medians in repeated measures data or matched groups.3. Tests for Association
    • Parametric counterpart: Pearson Correlation
    • Non-parametric equivalent: Spearman’s Rank Correlation
    • Assesses the relationship between two variables based on ranks.
    • Parametric counterpart: Linear Regression
    • Non-parametric equivalent: Theil-Sen Estimator or Rank-Based Regression
    • Estimates relationships without assuming linearity or normality.4. Tests for Goodness of Fit or Independence
    • Parametric counterpart: Chi-Square Test
    • Non-parametric equivalent: Chi-Square Test is itself non-parametric.
    • Used to test for relationships between categorical variables or to assess goodness of fit.Advantages of Non-Parametric Tests
    • Flexibility: Can be applied to data that are not interval or ratio-scaled.
    • Robustness: Less affected by outliers or non-normal distributions.
    • Simplicity: Often easier to apply and interpret for small datasets.Disadvantages of Non-Parametric Tests
    • Lower statistical power: Non-parametric tests are generally less powerful than parametric tests, meaning they are less likely to detect a true effect if one exists.
    • This is especially true when data are normal and meet parametric test assumptions.

    • Harder to interpret: Results are often reported in terms of ranks or medians, which can be less intuitive compared to means and standard deviations.SummaryNon-parametric tests are invaluable when working with non-normal, ordinal, or small datasets. While they are less powerful in detecting effects under ideal conditions (normal data, large sample sizes), they are much more robust to violations of assumptions, making them a go-to method when parametric assumptions cannot be met.
    9 what is Zero-Inflated Models ▸

    How Zero-Inflated Models Work

    A Zero-Inflated Model is used for datasets with excess zeros that cannot be adequately modeled by standard distributions (e.g., Poisson or Negative Binomial). It assumes that the zeros arise from two distinct processes:

    • A structural process that always generates zeros.
    • A random process that generates counts (including zeros) based on a standard distribution (e.g., Poisson or Negative Binomial).

    Key Components of a Zero-Inflated Model

    • Two Processes:
    • Zero-generation process: Determines whether a data point is a structural zero (always zero).
    • Modeled using logistic regression or similar techniques.
    • Count process: Governs counts (including zeros from random variability) using a distribution like Poisson or Negative Binomial.
    • Probabilities:
    • For each observation, there is a probability of being a structural zero.
    • If not a structural zero, the count is modeled using the count distribution.

    Steps in a Zero-Inflated Model

    • Determine Zero Inflation:
    • The model checks whether the observed data has more zeros than expected under a standard count distribution.
    • Fit Two Models Simultaneously:
    • A logistic regression model predicts whether an observation is a structural zero or not.
    • A count model (e.g., Poisson or Negative Binomial) predicts the counts for non-structural zeros.
    • Combine Results:
    • The final model combines both processes to estimate the likelihood of each observation.

    • Structural Zero: Patients who are generally healthy and will never visit the hospital.

    10 Mann-Whitney U Test ▸

    The Mann-Whitney U Test (also called the Wilcoxon Rank-Sum Test) is a non-parametric statistical test used to compare two independent groups to determine whether one group tends to have higher or lower values than the other. It evaluates whether the distributions of two groups differ without assuming normality.Key Features

    • Purpose: Test whether two independent groups have different distributions (medians, primarily).
    • Data requirements:
    • The dependent variable should be ordinal (ranked data) or continuous (non-normal is okay).
    • The independent variable must be categorical with two groups.
    • Assumptions:
    • The observations are independent of each other.
    • The dependent variable is measured on at least an ordinal scale.
    • The two groups have similar shapes/distributions (if comparing medians).How the Test WorksThe Mann-Whitney U test ranks all observations across both groups (from lowest to highest). Then, it compares the sum of the ranks for each group. If the two groups are from the same distribution, their ranks should be randomly distributed and have similar sums. A significant difference in rank sums indicates a difference between the groups.Steps:
    • Combine the data from both groups and rank all observations:
    • The smallest value gets a rank of 1, the next smallest a rank of 2, and so on.
    • If there are ties (same value), assign the average rank to those tied observations.
    • Calculate the rank sums for each group.Modern Implementation:Statistical software often calculates the p-value directly, making the test more user-friendly.When and Why to Use the Mann-Whitney U TestWhen to Use:
    • Non-Normal Data: Use the Mann-Whitney U Test when your data do not meet the normality assumption required for a two-sample t-test.
    • Ordinal Data: If your dependent variable is ordinal (e.g., Likert scales), this test is appropriate.
    • Small Sample Sizes: It is useful for small datasets where parametric tests might not perform well.
    • Outliers Present: The test is robust to outliers since it works with ranks instead of raw data.Why Use:
    • To compare two independent groups (e.g., male vs. female, treatment vs. control) without assuming normality.
    • To detect differences in medians or shifts in distributions.ExampleResearch Question:Do two teaching methods (Group A: traditional, Group B: online) lead to different exam scores?Data:

    • Cannot determine effect size or the exact nature of the difference (e.g., difference in variance, shape).
    • Assumes similar distributions across groups; if this is not true, the test might not compare medians effectively.SummaryThe Mann-Whitney U test is a robust, flexible alternative to a t-test for comparing two independent groups, particularly for non-normal or ordinal data.     11 structural zero ▸

    What is a Structural Zero?

    A structural zero refers to a situation where a zero value occurs in the data due to a structural or inherent reason in the process or system being studied. These zeros are not due to random chance or sampling variability but are a direct result of the nature of the data or context. Structural zeros are distinct from random zeros, which may occur due to natural variation or randomness in the data.

    Key Characteristics of Structural Zeros:

    • Not Random: Structural zeros are deterministic and occur because of specific conditions.
    • Cannot Change: They are fundamentally different from random zeros and will not change under repeated sampling.
    • Dependent on Context: They are tied to the nature of the system or rules of the study.

    Examples of Structural Zeros

    1. Demographics Example:

    • Scenario: A survey asks about the number of children in a household.
    • Structural Zero: Respondents who are single and do not live with children will always report "0 children." The zero is structural because the respondent is not in a position to have children in the household.

    2. Medical Studies Example:

    • Scenario: A study measures the number of visits to an obstetrician-gynecologist.
    • Structural Zero: Male participants will always report zero visits because they cannot biologically visit an obstetrician.

    3. Retail Example:

    • Scenario: An online store tracks the number of sales made by customers.
    • Structural Zero: People who registered but have not yet made any purchases will have a zero in the "sales" column.

    4. Manufacturing Example:

    • Scenario: A factory records the number of defective items for a production line.
    • Structural Zero: A machine that was not in use on a given day will report zero defects.

    Difference Between Structural Zeros and Random Zeros

    Why Are Structural Zeros Important?

    • Impact on Statistical Models:
    • Structural zeros violate the assumptions of many standard models (e.g., Poisson or Negative Binomial), which assume that zeros occur due to random processes.
    • Ignoring structural zeros can lead to biased parameter estimates or misleading conclusions.
    • Need for Specialized Models:
    • Models like Zero-Inflated Models or Hurdle Models explicitly account for structural zeros by separating the zero-generation process from the count-generation process.

    How to Handle Structural Zeros

    1. Identify the Source of Zeros:

    • Investigate whether zeros in your data are structural or random by understanding the context of the data collection process.

    2. Use Appropriate Models:

    • Zero-Inflated Models:
    • Combine a logistic regression (to model the structural zeros) and a count distribution (to model random zeros and counts).
    • Hurdle Models:
    • Separate the process of zero vs. non-zero from the process of modeling positive counts.

    3. Data Partitioning:

    • Partition the data into structural and non-structural subsets before analysis.

    4. Report Zeros Separately:

    • When reporting results, explicitly state the number and percentage of structural zeros in the dataset.

    Example of Modeling Structural Zeros

    Suppose you are analyzing customer purchase data, where many customers have never made a purchase (structural zeros), while others may have zero purchases due to random variability.

    • Zero-Inflated Poisson Model:
    • Logistic regression models the probability of being a structural zero.
    • Poisson regression models the count data (including random zeros and positive counts).

    12 Poisson Regression Models ▸

    Poisson Regression ModelsWhat Is Poisson Regression?Poisson regression is a type of generalized linear model (GLM) used to model count data or data that represent the number of occurrences of an event within a fixed period, space, or other units. It assumes that the dependent variable follows a Poisson distribution, which is suitable for modeling non-negative integers (0, 1, 2, …).

    The key characteristic of the Poisson distribution is that the mean (λ) and the variance are equal.

    When to Use Poisson RegressionSuitable for:

    • Count Data: When the dependent variable is a count (e.g., number of occurrences of an event).
    • Non-Negative Values: When the outcome variable cannot take negative values.
    • Over Discrete Time/Space Units: For modeling the rate of events within a specific timeframe or area.
    • Variance Close to Mean: Poisson regression assumes the variance is roughly equal to the mean. If this assumption is violated, alternative models (e.g., Negative Binomial Regression) should be considered.Examples of when to use:
    • Healthcare: Modeling the number of hospital visits for a patient in a year.
    • Marketing: Predicting the number of purchases made by a customer in a given period.
    • Transportation: Estimating the number of car accidents at a traffic intersection in a month.
    • Epidemiology: Modeling disease incidence (e.g., number of flu cases in a city per week).
    • Call Centers: Analyzing the number of customer service calls received per hour.

    Advantages of Poisson Regression

    • Handles Count Data: Designed specifically for modeling count-based outcomes.
    • Positive Predictions: Ensures predicted counts are non-negative.
    • Flexibility: Easily incorporates multiple predictors.
    • Interpretable Results: Coefficients are easy to interpret as rate changes or percentage effects.Limitations of Poisson Regression
    • Overdispersion: If the variance exceeds the mean, the model may underestimate standard errors, leading to overly optimistic p-values. Solutions:
    • Use Negative Binomial Regression to handle overdispersion.
    • Use robust standard errors.
    • Zero-Inflation: If there are too many zeros in the data, consider a Zero-Inflated Poisson model.
    • Independence Assumption: Assumes that counts are independent. If there’s clustering or correlation, other methods (e.g., hierarchical models) may be more appropriate.SummaryPoisson regression is a powerful tool for modeling count data, especially when the mean and variance are approximately equal. It uses a log-link function to predict rates or counts while ensuring positivity. Use it in scenarios with count outcomes, such as predicting traffic accidents, hospital visits, or customer interactions. If assumptions like overdispersion or excessive zeros arise, alternative models like Negative Binomial or Zero-Inflated Poisson should be explored.
