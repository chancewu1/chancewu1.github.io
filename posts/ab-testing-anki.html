    1 Statistical Significance and Practical Significance ▸

    In A/B testing, two key concepts to understand are statistical significance and practical significance. They both play important roles in determining whether an observed difference between groups is meaningful.Statistical Significance

    • Definition: Statistical significance refers to the likelihood that the observed difference between two groups (A and B) is not due to random chance. It is measured using a p-value, which indicates the probability of obtaining the observed results if the null hypothesis is true.
    • Criteria:
    • A result is considered statistically significant if the p-value is below a predefined threshold, often 0.05 (or 5%).
    • A low p-value suggests strong evidence against the null hypothesis, meaning the observed difference is unlikely to be due to chance.
    • Key Points:
    • Statistical significance does not measure the magnitude of the difference.
    • It depends on sample size: A larger sample size increases the power to detect small differences.Practical Significance
    • Definition: Practical significance evaluates whether the observed difference is large enough to be meaningful or relevant in a real-world context. It answers the question: "Is this difference worth acting upon?"
    • Criteria:
    • A result has practical significance if the difference has a meaningful impact on the business, user experience, or other goals.
    • The size of the effect is critical. For example, a 0.01% increase in conversion rate might not justify the cost of implementing the change.
    • Key Points:
    • Even if a result is statistically significant, it may not be practically significant.
    • Practical significance requires domain knowledge and judgment to interpret the impact.ExampleImagine you’re running an A/B test for a new button color on your website:
    • Statistical Significance: You find that the click-through rate for the new button (Group B) is 1.2% higher than the old button (Group A), with a p-value of 0.03. This indicates the difference is statistically significant.
    • Practical Significance: Despite being statistically significant, the 1.2% increase may not be meaningful for your business if the cost of redesigning and implementing the change outweighs the additional revenue generated by the increase in clicks.Conclusion
    • Statistical significance ensures the result is not due to chance.
    • Practical significance ensures the result is meaningful and worth acting on.
    • Both are necessary for making sound decisions in A/B testing.
    2 Size vs. Power Trade-Off? ▸

    The size vs. power trade-off refers to the relationship between the significance level (size of the test) and the power of a statistical test. Understanding this trade-off is crucial in designing and interpreting hypothesis tests, including A/B tests.

    Key Terms

    • Size of the Test (α):
    • The probability of making a Type I error (rejecting the null hypothesis when it is true).
    • Commonly denoted by α, often set at 0.05 (5% significance level).
    • Lower α reduces the risk of falsely identifying a difference that doesn't exist.
    • Power of the Test:
    • The probability of correctly rejecting the null hypothesis when the alternative hypothesis is true.
    • Power=1−β, where β is the probability of a Type II error (failing to reject the null hypothesis when it is false).
    • Higher power means the test is more likely to detect a true effect.
    • Statistics textbooks frequently define power to mean the same thing as sensitivity, that is, 1 - beta. However, conversationally power often means the probability that your test draws the correct conclusions, and this probability depends on both alpha and beta. In this course, we'll use the second definition, and we'll use sensitivity to refer to 1 - beta.
    • Type I and Type II Errors:
    • Type I error (False Positive): Detecting an effect when there isn’t one.
    • Type II error (False Negative): Failing to detect an effect when there is one.The Trade-Off
    • Lowering α (size):
    • Reduces the probability of a Type I error (fewer false positives).
    • Increases the probability of a Type II error, reducing the power of the test (more false negatives).
    • Increasing Power:
    • Reduces the probability of a Type II error (fewer false negatives).
    • Typically requires increasing the sample size or raising α, which might increase the chance of Type I errors.Factors Affecting the Trade-Off
    • Sample Size:
    • Larger sample sizes can improve power without increasing α.
    • A larger sample makes it easier to detect small differences between groups.
    • Effect Size:
    • A larger effect size naturally increases power because the difference is easier to detect.
    • Significance Level (α):
    • Choosing a smaller α (e.g., 0.01 instead of 0.05) decreases the chance of Type I errors but requires a larger sample to maintain the same power.
    • Variability in Data:
    • Higher variability reduces power. Reducing noise or increasing measurement precision can improve power.Balancing the Trade-Off
    • Context and Stakes:
    • If the cost of a Type I error is high (e.g., approving an ineffective drug), use a smaller α.
    • If the cost of a Type II error is high (e.g., missing a life-saving treatment), prioritize power.
    • Sample Size:
    • Increasing sample size reduces the need to compromise between α and power, as it improves the test's ability to detect true effects without increasing the risk of Type I errors.
    • Pre-study Power Analysis:
    • Conduct a power analysis to determine the required sample size for detecting a meaningful effect with a given significance level.Example
    • You’re testing whether a new website design increases conversions:
    • Setting α=0.01: You reduce the risk of falsely declaring the new design better, but you may miss real improvements unless you increase your sample size.
    • Setting α=0.1: You’re more likely to detect a real improvement but at the risk of more false positives.By adjusting the sample size or accepting a moderate trade-off, you can balance the risk of both types of errors.
    3 what is the difference between power, sensitivity, alpha and beta in AB testing ▸

    In A/B testing, power, sensitivity, alpha, and beta are critical concepts that define the reliability and accuracy of your experiment. Here's a breakdown of each:1. Power:

    • Definition: The probability of correctly rejecting the null hypothesis when it is false (i.e., detecting a true effect when there is one).
    • Formula: Power=1−β
    • Importance: High power (typically 80% or 90%) ensures that the test is sensitive enough to detect meaningful changes.
    • Interpretation: If your test has 80% power, there's an 80% chance you'll detect a real difference if it exists.2. Sensitivity:
    • Definition: The ability of a test to detect a difference or effect when one exists. It is closely related to power.
    • Importance: Higher sensitivity increases the likelihood of identifying small, yet significant, effects.
    • Relation to Power: Sensitivity can be thought of as a broader term encompassing the test's ability to detect changes, while power specifically refers to detecting true positives.3. Alpha (α):
    • Definition: The significance level, representing the probability of a Type I error (rejecting the null hypothesis when it is actually true).
    • Typical Value: Commonly set at 0.05 (5%), meaning there's a 5% chance of a false positive.
    • Importance: Controls the likelihood of detecting an effect due to random chance rather than a true difference.
    • Interpretation: A lower alpha reduces the risk of false positives but requires a larger sample size to maintain power.4. Beta (β):
    • Definition: The probability of a Type II error (failing to reject the null hypothesis when it is actually false).
    • Formula: β=1−Power
    • Importance: A smaller beta means a lower chance of missing a true effect.
    • Interpretation: If beta is 20%, there's a 20% chance you'll miss a real difference (false negative).Interrelationships:
    • Power vs. Alpha: Lower alpha (more stringent test) reduces the likelihood of Type I error but can reduce power unless the sample size is increased.
    • Beta vs. Power: Beta is inversely related to power. Higher power means a lower chance of missing an effect.
    • Sensitivity vs. Alpha and Beta: Sensitivity increases when alpha and beta are balanced appropriately and sample size is sufficient.
    • Practical Example:
    • If you set alpha at 0.05, power at 80%, and observe no significant result, there's a 5% chance of a false positive (Type I error) and a 20% chance of a false negative (Type II error).In summary, these terms are interdependent and together influence the reliability and accuracy of your A/B test conclusions. Balancing them involves trade-offs between risk of errors, sample size, and the ability to detect meaningful effects.

    1. Sensitivity: Detecting Changes

    A sensitive metric quickly and accurately reflects the impact of changes in the system. It should respond to the changes you're testing without requiring an overly large sample size.Key Considerations for Sensitivity:

    • Granularity: Choose metrics that capture fine-grained changes.
    • Example: Use "click-through rate" (CTR) instead of "total revenue" for an experiment testing button designs.
    • Signal-to-Noise Ratio: Look for metrics with low variability unrelated to the treatment.
    • Example: Revenue per user (RPU) might be more sensitive than total revenue because it normalizes user activity.
    • Direct Impact: Metrics should closely tie to the change you’re testing.
    • Example: If you change a login page, measure "login success rate," not "daily active users."
    • Time to Reflect Change: Choose metrics that update quickly after changes.
    • Example: Measure daily retention instead of monthly retention for short-term experiments.2. Robustness: Handling Noise and BiasA robust metric remains stable and reliable in the presence of random variations, user heterogeneity, or external noise.Key Considerations for Robustness:
    • Stability Over Time: Ensure the metric isn’t overly influenced by external factors.
    • Example: "Average session duration" may be unstable if user behavior varies due to holidays or promotions.
    • Independence from Sample Bias: Avoid metrics skewed by specific user groups or outliers.
    • Example: Use the median instead of the mean to reduce the effect of extreme values in purchase amounts.
    • Scalability Across Samples: Ensure the metric behaves consistently across different subsets of data.
    • Example: A metric like "percent of users completing a task" works better across cohorts than "raw completion counts."
    • Minimal External Confounding: Control for or exclude metrics that are influenced by factors outside your experiment.
    • Example: Revenue might be confounded by marketing campaigns during the experiment.
    8 what is summary metrics ▸

    Summary metrics in A/B testing are high-level, aggregated measurements that provide a concise way to evaluate the performance of your test groups (control and treatment). They distill the data into key numbers that reflect the success or failure of your test, making it easier to interpret and draw actionable insights.Characteristics of Summary Metrics

    • Aggregated: Combine data points across users or sessions (e.g., average revenue per user).
    • Directly Linked to Business Goals: Reflect key outcomes like revenue, engagement, or retention.
    • Actionable: Help determine whether to adopt, reject, or iterate on a feature or change.
    • Sensitive and Robust: Capture meaningful differences between control and treatment while minimizing noise.Types of Summary Metrics1. Business Metrics
    • Represent the core objectives of the business.
    • Examples:
    • Revenue per user (RPU): Measures how much revenue is generated on average per user.
    • Conversion rate: Percentage of users completing a desired action (e.g., purchase, signup).2. Behavioral Metrics
    • Measure user engagement or interactions with the product.
    • Examples:
    • Click-through rate (CTR): Percentage of users clicking a specific button or link.
    • Time on page/session: Average time users spend on a page or during a session.
    • Task completion rate: Percentage of users successfully completing a task.3. Performance Metrics
    • Assess the technical impact of changes.
    • Examples:
    • Page load time: Average time taken for a page to load.
    • Error rate: Percentage of failed actions (e.g., failed logins, broken links).4. Retention Metrics
    • Track how well users stick with your product over time.
    • Examples:
    • Daily/Weekly Active Users (DAU/WAU): Number of users engaging with the product regularly.
    • Retention rate: Percentage of users returning after a specific period.Why Use Summary Metrics?
    • Simplify Decision-Making: Instead of looking at individual data points, summary metrics aggregate the results into easy-to-understand numbers.
    • Highlight Trends: They reveal whether the treatment outperforms the control in key areas.
    • Ensure Alignment: Focus on metrics that align with business or product goals.
    • Measure Trade-offs: Guardrail metrics ensure that the change does not negatively affect other areas (e.g., faster page loads might increase bounce rates).How to Choose Good Summary Metrics
    • Aligned to the Objective: Ensure the metric reflects what you want to optimize.
    • Example: For a checkout process, use "conversion rate" rather than "time on site."
    • Sensitive to Changes: Pick metrics that quickly detect meaningful differences.
    • Example: "Revenue per user" is more sensitive than "total revenue."
    • Robust to Noise: Use metrics that aren't overly influenced by random fluctuations or outliers.
    • Example: Median values often work better than averages for metrics like session duration.
    • Easy to Interpret: Ensure stakeholders can easily understand the metric.
    • Example: "Click-through rate" is more intuitive than a complex weighted metric.Example of Summary Metrics in A/B TestingScenario: Testing a New Checkout Flow
    • Primary Metric (Business Goal): Conversion rate (percentage of users completing a purchase).
    • Behavioral Metrics: Time to complete checkout, drop-off rate during checkout.
    • Performance Metrics: Page load time during checkout.
    • Guardrail Metrics: Bounce rate on the checkout page (ensure changes don’t hurt overall engagement).Common Pitfalls with Summary Metrics
    • Over-Focusing on One Metric: Ignoring secondary or guardrail metrics can lead to biased conclusions.
    • Example: A higher click-through rate might decrease conversion if users are misled.
    • Ignoring Context: A good metric for one test might not work for another.
    • Example: "Revenue per user" might not be ideal for testing onboarding flows.
    • Sensitivity vs. Robustness Trade-Off: Highly sensitive metrics can be noisy, while highly robust metrics may miss smaller changes.SummarySummary metrics in A/B testing are the aggregated measures used to evaluate test results and make decisions. They should:
    • Align with business goals.
    • Be sensitive enough to detect meaningful changes.
    • Be robust to noise and outliers.
    • Be simple and interpretable for all stakeholders.

    11 Variablity in metircs in ab testing ▸

    variability in A/B testing metrics refers to the inherent fluctuations in the observed performance metrics due to randomness, differences in user behavior, or sampling errors. Understanding this variability is crucial for making informed decisions based on the results of an A/B test.Here’s a breakdown of key aspects related to variability in A/B testing metrics:1. Sources of Variability

    • User Behavior: Individual user preferences and interactions with your system can vary widely.
    • Seasonality: Time-dependent effects such as holidays or day-of-week variations can influence results.
    • Random Sampling: Assigning users to control or variant groups randomly introduces natural differences.
    • External Factors: Uncontrollable variables like economic conditions or competitor actions can affect user behavior.2. Impact of Variability
    • Confidence Intervals: Variability affects the width of confidence intervals for estimated metrics (e.g., click-through rate or conversion rate).
    • Significance Levels: High variability can make it harder to detect statistically significant differences.
    • False Positives/Negatives: Misinterpreting variability can lead to Type I errors (false positives) or Type II errors (false negatives).3. Key Metrics Affected by Variability
    • Conversion Rate: Variability is often high because conversion events are binary and relatively rare.
    • Click-Through Rate (CTR): Click behavior varies significantly across users.
    • Revenue Metrics: Metrics like average order value can show high variability due to outliers.
    • Engagement Metrics: Time spent or pages viewed may be influenced by outliers or user preferences.4. Measuring and Managing Variability
    • Sample Size: Larger sample sizes reduce variability and provide more stable estimates.
    • Bootstrapping: Resampling techniques can help understand the distribution of metrics and quantify variability.
    • Segmentation: Analyzing subsets of data (e.g., by geography or user type) can reveal patterns masked by overall variability.
    • Bayesian Methods: Bayesian A/B testing incorporates prior information and can handle variability more robustly than frequentist methods.5. Statistical Techniques to Address Variability
    • Confidence Intervals: Provide a range within which the true metric is likely to lie, accounting for variability.
    • t-Tests/Z-Tests: Evaluate differences while considering sample variability.
    • Variance Reduction Techniques: Use paired testing, covariate adjustment, or stratified randomization to minimize variability.
    • Sequential Testing: Reduces the risk of incorrect decisions by dynamically monitoring test results.6. Best Practices
    • Pre-Test Power Analysis: Ensure your test has enough statistical power to detect meaningful differences given the expected variability.
    • Run Tests Long Enough: Allow tests to capture representative variability across different user segments and time periods.
    • Monitor External Factors: Account for changes in traffic sources, marketing campaigns, or external events.
    • Use Robust Metrics: Consider metrics less sensitive to outliers, such as median values or trimmed means.7. Visualizing Variability
    • Histograms: Display the distribution of observed metrics across samples.
    • Box Plots: Show variability and potential outliers in a compact format.
