    1 What is Normal Distribution and examples ▸

    The normal distribution, also known as the Gaussian distribution, is a probability distribution that is symmetric about the mean. In a normal distribution, most of the observations cluster around the central peak, and the probability of observing values decreases as you move away from the mean. The shape of the distribution is bell-shaped and characterized by two parameters:

    • Mean (μ): The central value around which the distribution is centered.
    • Standard Deviation (σ): A measure of the spread or dispersion of the values.Real-Life Examples:
    • Height of people: In most populations, the distribution of heights follows a normal distribution, with most people being of average height and fewer people being very short or very tall.
    • Test scores: In large groups of students taking the same exam, the distribution of scores tends to be normal, with most students scoring around the average and fewer scoring very low or very high.
    • Measurement errors: In scientific experiments or manufacturing processes, errors in measurements often follow a normal distribution due to random factors.

    3 What is Poisson Distribution and examples? ▸

    The Poisson distribution models the number of times an event occurs in a fixed interval of time or space, given the average rate at which the event happens. It is used for counting events that happen independently and at a constant average rate. The distribution is discrete, meaning it counts occurrences of events, and is typically used when events are rare or happen in random intervals.Real-Life Examples:

    • Number of phone calls at a call center: If a call center receives an average of 10 calls per hour, the Poisson distribution can be used to model the probability of receiving exactly 8 or exactly 12 calls in an hour.
    • Traffic accidents: The number of traffic accidents at a specific intersection in a given period can be modeled using a Poisson distribution if accidents happen at a relatively constant average rate.
    • Number of emails received: The number of emails a person receives per hour can be modeled with a Poisson distribution if the emails arrive at random intervals, and the average rate of email arrivals is known.

    8 What is Binomial Distribution and examples ▸

    The binomial distribution is a discrete probability distribution that models the number of successes in a fixed number of independent trials of a binary experiment. Each trial has only two possible outcomes: success or failure. The probability of success is the same for each trial. The distribution is characterized by two parameters:

    • n: The number of trials.
    • p: The probability of success on a single trial.The binomial distribution gives the probability of having exactly k successes in n trials. It is often used when you have a situation where you're repeating the same experiment multiple times.Real-Life Examples:
    • Coin flipping: If you flip a fair coin 10 times, the binomial distribution can model the probability of getting exactly 6 heads. Here, n = 10 trials (flips), and p = 0.5 (probability of heads).
    • Quality control: If a factory produces 100 items, and the probability of an item being defective is 0.02, the binomial distribution can be used to determine the probability that exactly 5 items will be defective.
    • Survey responses: If a company surveys 50 customers, and the probability that a customer will recommend their service is 0.3, the binomial distribution can model the probability that exactly 15 customers will recommend the service.

    A t-test is a statistical method used to determine whether there is a significant difference between the means of two groups, or between a sample mean and a known population mean. It is commonly used when the sample size is small and the population standard deviation is unknown.Types of t-tests:

    • One-sample t-test: Tests if the mean of a single sample is significantly different from a known value or population mean.
    • Independent (two-sample) t-test: Compares the means of two independent groups to determine if they are significantly different.
    • Paired t-test: Compares the means of two related groups (e.g., before-and-after measurements) to assess changes within the same group.What does the t-value tell us?The t-value is a calculated statistic that measures the size of the difference relative to the variation in the sample data. It is essentially a ratio:
    • A large t-value indicates that the groups are significantly different.
    • A small t-value suggests that the groups are not significantly different.Key points about the t-value:
    • Magnitude:
    • A higher t-value implies greater evidence against the null hypothesis (i.e., the observed difference is not due to random chance).
    • A lower t-value suggests weaker evidence against the null hypothesis.
    • Sign and Direction:
    • A positive t-value indicates the sample mean is greater than the hypothesized mean (or the first group mean is greater than the second in a two-sample t-test).
    • A negative t-value indicates the opposite.
    • Comparison to Critical Value: The t-value is compared to a critical value (from a t-distribution table) at a specific confidence level (e.g., 95%) and degrees of freedom (related to sample size). If the t-value exceeds the critical value, the null hypothesis is rejected.Interpreting Results:
    • p-value: Most modern analyses report the p-value alongside the t-value. The p-value indicates the probability of observing the data, or something more extreme, if the null hypothesis is true. A low p-value (typically < 0.05) suggests rejecting the null hypothesis.
    • Degrees of Freedom (df): This impacts the shape of the t-distribution and depends on sample size. It’s used to look up the critical value for the t-distribution.Practical Example:
    • Scenario: You want to test if a new teaching method improves student test scores compared to the standard method.
    • Approach: Use an independent t-test comparing scores from two groups (students taught by the new method vs. the standard method).
    • Outcome: A large positive or negative t-value (with a small p-value) would suggest the new method has a significant impact.

    20 Relationship between Confidence Interval and P-Value: ▸

    Yes, the confidence interval and the p-value (commonly set to 0.05) are closely related, as they both deal with statistical inference and uncertainty in decision-making.Relationship between Confidence Interval and P-Value:

    • P-Value (e.g., 0.05):
    • The p-value measures the strength of evidence against the null hypothesis (H0​) in a statistical test.
    • When we set the significance level (α) at 0.05, it means we are willing to accept a 5% chance of rejecting the null hypothesis when it is actually true (Type I error).
    • If the p-value is less than 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis because the result is considered statistically significant.
    • Confidence Interval (e.g., 95%):
    • A 95% confidence interval corresponds to a significance level (α) of 0.05 in hypothesis testing.
    • If a confidence interval does not contain the null hypothesis value (e.g., 0 in a mean difference test), the result is statistically significant, and the p-value will be less than 0.05.Example to Link the Two:Imagine you are testing whether a new drug is effective compared to a placebo. The null hypothesis is that the drug has no effect (H0:μ=0) If the 95% confidence interval for the drug’s effect is (1.2, 3.5):
    • The interval does not include 0 (the null hypothesis value), so we reject the null hypothesis.
    • The corresponding p-value would be less than 0.05.
    • If the confidence interval is (-0.8, 2.3):
    • The interval includes 0, so we fail to reject the null hypothesis.
    • The p-value would be greater than 0.05.Summary of the Link:
    • Confidence Level (e.g., 95%) and Significance Level (α=0.05) are complements (1−α).
    • A result is statistically significant (p-value < 0.05) if the confidence interval excludes the null hypothesis value.

    The p-value is a statistical measure that helps determine the significance of results in both linear regression and A/B testing. Here's how it's used in each context:P-value in Linear RegressionIn linear regression, the p-value is used to determine whether an independent variable significantly predicts the dependent variable.     Key Points:

    • Alternative Hypothesis (H₁): The predictor variable has an impact on the outcome (coefficient ≠ 0).
    • Interpretation:
    • If the p-value < chosen significance level (e.g., 0.05), you reject the null hypothesis, suggesting that the variable is statistically significant.
    • If the p-value ≥ 0.05, you fail to reject the null hypothesis, indicating no significant relationship.Example:For a predictor variable (e.g., "Years of Experience") in a regression model:
    • Coefficient: 0.5
    • p-value: 0.03
    • Interpretation: The p-value is less than 0.05, so "Years of Experience" significantly impacts the outcome.P-value in A/B TestingIn A/B testing, the p-value helps evaluate whether the observed difference between two groups (e.g., Group A and Group B) is due to random chance or a true effect.

    41 What is selection bias ▸

    Selection bias is a type of error that occurs when the participants or data included in a study, survey, or analysis are not representative of the larger population intended to be analyzed. This bias arises from the method used to select individuals or groups, leading to systematic differences between those included in the study and those who are not. As a result, the findings may not accurately reflect the true characteristics, behaviors, or outcomes of the entire population.

For example, analyzing successful businesses without considering those that failed can lead to misleading conclusions about what contributes to success. 4. Attrition Bias: • Occurs in longitudinal studies when participants drop out over time, potentially altering the study’s outcome if the dropout is related to the study variables.

    Examples of Selection Bias: • Medical Research: If a clinical trial only includes participants from a specific geographic area, the results may not be generalizable to other regions with different demographics. • Employment Studies: Surveying only employed individuals to understand job satisfaction can miss perspectives from those who are unemployed, leading to biased conclusions. • Political Polling: Relying solely on phone surveys may exclude younger individuals who primarily use mobile devices or online platforms, skewing the results.

    Consequences of Selection Bias: • Invalid Conclusions: Decisions or theories based on biased data may be flawed, leading to ineffective or harmful policies and practices. • Reduced Credibility: Research affected by selection bias may lose credibility among peers and the public, undermining trust in the findings. • Misallocation of Resources: Resources may be directed based on inaccurate data, leading to inefficiencies and missed opportunities.

    Example:If you survey 100 voters out of a population of 10,000, the proportion of voters favoring a particular candidate in your sample might slightly differ from the true proportion in the entire population purely by chance.

    • For a single sample:
    • To test whether the mean of a sample is significantly different from the population mean.

    • To test whether two independent sample means are significantly different.

    • For proportions:
    • To test differences in proportions between two groups.
    • Example: Comparing the proportion of people who prefer two different brands.Types of Z-Tests

    Key Takeaways

    • Z-tests are great for large sample sizes and when the population variance is known.
    • For small sample sizes or unknown population variance, a t-test is typically more appropriate.
    • Z-tests are commonly applied in quality control, A/B testing, and hypothesis testing in various fields.

    A T-Test is a statistical test used to determine if there is a significant difference between:

    • The mean of a sample and a known population mean (one-sample T-Test).
    • The means of two independent groups (two-sample T-Test).
    • The means of paired samples (paired T-Test).Unlike a Z-Test, a T-Test is used when the sample size is small (n<30) or when the population variance (standard deviation) is unknown.

    Types of T-Tests

    • One-Sample T-Test:
    • Compares the mean of a single sample to a known or hypothesized population mean.
    • Example: Testing if the average weight of a group differs from 70kg.
    • Two-Sample (Independent) T-Test:
    • Compares the means of two independent groups to see if there is a significant difference.
    • Example: Comparing test scores between students from two different schools.

    • Compares the means of two related groups (e.g., "before" and "after" measurements for the same group).

    • The data is independent (for independent T-tests).
    • For two-sample T-tests, the variances of the two groups are either equal (pooled) or unequal (Welch's T-Test).

    • Less prone to overfitting than individual models.Disadvantages:
    • Does not significantly reduce bias.
    • Relies on strong base learners for better performance.2. BoostingBoosting reduces both bias and variance by sequentially training models, where each model tries to correct the errors of its predecessor.How it works:
    • Sequential Training:
    • Train the first model on the entire dataset.
    • Assign higher weights to misclassified examples or underperforming regions.
    • Iterative Improvement:
    • Train the next model to focus more on the errors made by the previous model.
    • Combine predictions from all models to improve overall accuracy.
    • Aggregation:
    • Final prediction is a weighted sum or majority vote of all models, with greater weight given to better-performing models.Key Characteristics:
    • Models are trained sequentially, not independently.
    • Focuses on reducing bias and improving overall performance.
    • Common algorithms: AdaBoost, Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM), XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost.Advantages:
    • Often achieves higher accuracy than bagging by reducing bias.
    • Works well with weak learners (e.g., shallow decision trees).
    • Can handle complex datasets with intricate decision boundaries.Disadvantages:
    • More prone to overfitting, especially with noisy data.
    • Computationally intensive due to sequential training.
    • Harder to parallelize compared to bagging.When to Use Bagging or Boosting
    • Bagging:
    • When the primary issue is high variance or overfitting.
    • For models that are unstable (e.g., decision trees).
    • Datasets with less noise or outliers.
    • Example: Random Forest is widely used for classification and regression tasks.
    • Boosting:
    • When the model suffers from high bias or underfitting.
    • For improving weak learners and tackling complex relationships.
    • On clean datasets with minimal noise or outliers.
    • Example: XGBoost and LightGBM are highly popular for structured data in competitions like Kaggle.
