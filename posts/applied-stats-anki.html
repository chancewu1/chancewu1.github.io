    1 Basic Statistics- [General - Easy] Mean, Median, Percentile When would you use the mean, median and percentile? ▸

    All three are statistical measures of data. When describing the centrality of data distribution, you can use the mean and median (= 50th percentile). If the distribution is normal, then either one is fine. However, in the presence of outlier or skewness, the mean is sensitive toward the extreme values. On the other hand, the median is a robust measure against the extremes. So, you would want to use the median if the shape of the distribution is skewed or present with outliers.

    Finally, you would want to use percentile if you want to gauge the lower and upper bound of the distribution. Suppose that you want to understand observations that are generally considered low or high, then you would want to look at the 10th percentile or 90th percentile of the distribution, respectively.

    3 What is Anova and why use it? ▸

    What is ANOVA?ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) is a statistical method used to determine if there are statistically significant differences between the means of three or more independent groups. It compares the variability within groups to the variability between groups to assess whether the observed differences in group means could have occurred by chance.

    The core concept of ANOVA is that it uses two different variance estimates to assess whether group means differ. The technique partitions the overall variance among all of the observations into between-groups and within-groups variance to evaluate whether the samples might have come from the same underlying population

    • we can simultaneously analyze the differences in means among any number of groups.
    • comparing many diff groups without having to repeat individual t-tests over and over again.
    • we can examine combinations of different factors(eg, colors in webpage, font sizes)
    • It is a general liner model(GLM) technique
    • a set of independent variables, also often called predictors, are related to or used to predict a dependent variable, also called an outcome variable.
    • We can learn a few things from ANOVA

    Single effect size: the overall quality of the statistical result(the strength or quality of prediction)

    The inferential statistical test on both model quality and on the the each of coefficient:

    To test the hypothesis that the statistical value(eg: R-squares) is significantly different from zero.

    To establish a likely range of values(confidential Interval)

    To show the likelihoods associated with different values(Bayes test)

    One coefficient for each independent variables, theses coefficients are used in a linear

    additive equation to model/predict the dependent variables.

    Diagnostic information to make sure that all of the necessary assumptions about the data are satisfied; for example, that the error values from

    the predictions are normally distributed\

    Why Use ANOVA?

    • Comparing Multiple Groups Simultaneously

    ANOVA allows you to compare three or more groups at once, rather than performing multiple pairwise ttt-tests, which would increase the risk of a Type I error (false positive).

    • Identifying Statistical Significance

    It helps identify whether the differences in group means are statistically significant or due to random variation.

    • Handling Variability

    ANOVA separates total variability into variability due to differences between groups and variability within groups, providing a structured analysis.

    • Testing Hypotheses

    It tests the null hypothesis (H0​) that all group means are equal against the alternative hypothesis (HaH_aHa​) that at least one group mean is different.When to Use ANOVA?

    • You have one independent variable (factor) with three or more levels (e.g., different diets, treatments, or teaching methods).
    • You want to analyze the effect of the independent variable on a continuous dependent variable (e.g., weight loss, test scores, or reaction time).Types of ANOVA
    • One-Way ANOVA: Used when there is one independent variable.
    • Two-Way ANOVA: Used when there are two independent variables to assess their interaction effects.
    • Repeated Measures ANOVA: Used when the same subjects are measured multiple times under different conditions.ExampleSuppose you are testing the effectiveness of three different fertilizers on plant growth. ANOVA helps determine if the differences in plant growth between the three fertilizers are statistically significant or just due to natural variation.

    Key TakeawayANOVA is a powerful tool in experimental analysis to determine whether there are meaningful differences among group means, and it is essential for maintaining the validity of statistical conclusions when comparing multiple groups.

    Core Concept of ANOVA

    • Variance Estimates:
    • ANOVA relies on two key variance estimates:
    • Between-Groups Variance: Reflects the variability of group means around the overall mean (measuring differences due to treatment effects or predictors).
    • Within-Groups Variance: Captures the variability of observations within each group (assumed to be due to random error or inherent variation).By comparing these variances, ANOVA assesses whether the group means are significantly different, implying the groups may come from distinct populations.Why Use ANOVA?
    • Simultaneous Analysis of Multiple Groups:
    • ANOVA can compare the means of multiple groups in one test without the need for repeated ttt-tests, thus minimizing the risk of Type I errors (false positives).
    • Combining Multiple Factors:
    • ANOVA can handle combinations of different factors (e.g., colors and font sizes on a webpage) to analyze their individual and interaction effects on the outcome variable.
    • General Linear Model (GLM) Foundation:
    • ANOVA is part of the General Linear Model (GLM), which establishes relationships between:
    • Predictors (independent variables) and
    • Outcome (dependent variable).
    • This allows flexible analysis of continuous, categorical, or interaction terms.Practical Use Cases
    • Experimental Design:
    • Evaluating treatment effects across multiple groups in clinical trials.
    • Marketing Optimization:
    • Analyzing combinations of webpage designs (colors, fonts, layouts) to maximize user engagement.
    • Quality Control:
    • Testing whether multiple production methods result in consistent product quality.Why GLM Matters in ANOVA
    • ANOVA models are special cases of GLM:
    • It assumes the outcome variable is continuous and normally distributed.
    • The independent variables can be categorical (e.g., treatments, groups) or continuous (e.g., dose levels).Example:Suppose you are studying the effect of font size (Factor A) and color scheme (Factor B) on website click-through rates (Outcome). Using Two-Way ANOVA, you can:
    • Assess the main effects of font size and color scheme individually.
    • Investigate the interaction effect between font size and color scheme.Key Takeaway:ANOVA’s power lies in its ability to partition and analyze variance systematically, allowing researchers to test hypotheses about group means and predictor-outcome relationships efficiently within the robust GLM framework.
    4 Differences between the ANOVA and other techniques ▸

    The only difference between ANOVA and these other techniques is that the independent variables in ANOVA tend to be categorical rather than metric.

    • Categorical: the variable indicates membership in two or more discrete categories.
    • Metric : the variable ascertains an ordered measurement of some type, such as ratings on a 10-point scale, weight, or number of bytes of memory.

    5 what is within-group and between groups in Anova? ▸

    In ANOVA, variance is a key concept used to assess whether the means of different groups are statistically different. The variance is partitioned into within-group variance and between-group variance to understand the sources of variability in the data.1. Within-Group Variance

    • Definition: Variability of observations within each group.
    • Cause: Represents the differences among individual data points in the same group, typically due to random variation or measurement error.
    • Purpose: Provides a baseline measure of how much variability exists naturally within each group.
    • Calculation:
    • Compute the variance for each group.
    • Average these variances (weighted by group size).Example:If you are comparing test scores from three different classes, the within-group variance captures how much individual scores differ within each class.2. Between-Group Variance
    • Definition: Variability of the group means relative to the overall (grand) mean.
    • Cause: Reflects differences in group means, which may be due to the effect of the independent variable (e.g., treatment or group differences).
    • Purpose: Measures the extent to which the groups differ from each other.
    • Calculation:
    • Compute the difference between each group mean and the overall mean.
    • Weight this difference by the size of each group.Example:In the same test score example, the between-group variance captures how much the average scores of the three classes differ from the overall average score.Key Relationship in ANOVAThe total variance in the data can be expressed as:
    • High Between-Group Variance relative to Within-Group Variance indicates that the group means are likely significantly different.
    • Low Between-Group Variance relative to Within-Group Variance suggests that group differences may be due to random variation.How ANOVA Uses These VariancesANOVA compares the between-group variance to the within-group variance using the F-statistic:
    • MSB (Mean Square Between): Average variance due to group differences.
    • MSW (Mean Square Within): Average variance within groups.If the FFF-statistic is significantly large, it suggests that the group means are not all equal, and at least one group differs significantly.Summary
    • Within-Group Variance: Variability due to random differences within each group (noise).
    • Between-Group Variance: Variability due to differences in group means (signal).
    • ANOVA uses the ratio of between-group variance to within-group variance to determine if the group means differ significantly.

    grouping variable. This formula might seem very familiar because it is at the heart

    This formula calculates the squared deviation between each group mean and the grand mean and adds the results together.

    more clearly, using (number of observations in that group (n)) (means in each group- grand mean from population)^2 and sum them togather.*

    That scaling factor n puts the between-groups variability on the same footing as the within groups variability.

    if you add together the sum-of- squares within groups to the sum-of-squares between groups, you will get the total sum-of-squares again

    We just partitioned the total variability in the data set into two components

    • the variability represented inside of each group (summed across groups)
    • the variability among the group means (with the appropriate scaling factor).

    The numerator of the ratio is the scaled variance among the means of the groups—between- groups variance.

    The denominator of the ratio is the variance within the groups (pooled together to form a single estimate)—naturally called the within-groups variance.

    8 Does a Log Transformation Help Achieve Normality? and when you can use it ▸

    Does a Log Transformation Help Achieve Normality?A logarithmic transformation can sometimes make a non-normal distribution closer to normal, but it doesn’t guarantee a perfect normal distribution. It is particularly useful for data that is:

    • Right-skewed: When the distribution has a long tail on the right (e.g., income, wait times, click-through rates).
    • Multiplicative relationships: When the relationship between variables is multiplicative rather than additive.When to Use Log Transformation
    • When the data is skewed:
    • If the data has extreme positive outliers or a long right tail, taking the log (or natural log) compresses large values while spreading smaller values, reducing skewness.
    • Example: Transforming income data where a few very high earners skew the mean.
    • When variances are unequal:
    • A log transformation can stabilize variances, especially when larger values have larger variability.
    • For positive, non-zero data:
    • Log transformations are only defined for positive values (x>0x > 0x>0). If the data contains zeros or negatives, a constant may need to be added before applying the transformation (e.g., log⁡(x+c)\log(x + c)log(x+c)).
    • To linearize relationships:
    • If two variables have a nonlinear relationship (e.g., exponential or power-law), log transformations can make them linear, which simplifies regression modeling.When Log Transformation Doesn't Work
    • When the data includes zero or negative values:
    • Logarithms are undefined for x≤0x \leq 0x≤0. Transformations like log⁡(x+c)\log(x + c)log(x+c) (where c>0c > 0c>0) might work but can distort the scale if ccc is large.
    • For left-skewed data:
    • A log transformation can worsen left-skewed data because it compresses smaller values further.
    • If the data isn't skewed:
    • Applying a log transformation to symmetric or already-normal data can distort it unnecessarily.
    • When extreme outliers dominate:
    • While a log transformation reduces the influence of outliers, it won't "fix" data where a few points are vastly larger than the rest.
    • When interpretability matters:
    • Transformed data can be harder to interpret in its transformed scale, especially for non-technical audiences.Practical ExampleBefore Log Transformation:Suppose you have data on income ($): [10,50,100,5000,10000][10, 50, 100, 5000, 10000][10,50,100,5000,10000]. This dataset is highly right-skewed.
    • Mean: 3,232 (heavily influenced by outliers).
    • Median: 100 (a better central measure due to skewness).After Log Transformation:Take the natural log (ln⁡\lnln) of the income: [ln⁡(10),ln⁡(50),ln⁡(100),ln⁡(5000),ln⁡(10000)][\ln(10), \ln(50), \ln(100), \ln(5000), \ln(10000)][ln(10),ln(50),ln(100),ln(5000),ln(10000)].
    • Transformed values: [2.3,3.9,4.6,8.5,9.2][2.3, 3.9, 4.6, 8.5, 9.2][2.3,3.9,4.6,8.5,9.2].
    • The transformed data is more symmetrical and closer to normal.Steps to Check if a Log Transformation Works
    • Visual Inspection:
    • Plot histograms or density plots of the data before and after transformation to check if skewness is reduced.
    • Statistical Tests:
    • Use tests like the Shapiro-Wilk or Kolmogorov-Smirnov test to assess normality before and after transformation.
    • Evaluate Model Performance:
    • If using the log-transformed data improves residuals or stabilizes variance, it’s a sign the transformation is appropriate.Alternatives to Log TransformationIf the log transformation doesn’t work or isn’t applicable, consider:
    • Square Root Transformation: Reduces skewness but less aggressive than logs.
    • Box-Cox Transformation: A flexible power transformation that includes log as a special case.
    • Rank-Based Methods: Use nonparametric methods that don’t assume normality.Summary
    • When it works: Right-skewed data, positive values, variance stabilization, nonlinear relationships.
    • When it doesn’t work: Zero/negative values, left-skewed data, extreme outliers, when interpretability is critical.
